On the Origin of Mind

A Matter of Senses, and Time

Short Version

In Nuce.

Minkowski mind space, mink space in short, comprises all sensory axes of an organism plus time. The observer (organism) is located in mink space origin where its worldline intersects the present plane. The present plane separates the future from the past. Events are characterized by a certain pattern in mink space. Organisms are specialists in identifying such patterns. Event patterns can be static, dynamic, or complex patterns. An object is a static pattern (e.g. an animal or written word), motion (e.g. rotation, a spoken word, or sign language) is a dynamic pattern, and combined events (e.g. 'writing a word' or 'coming home') are complex patterns. There are no limits to how complex an event pattern can become. Events in the same time plane are called horizontal; event parallel to the time axis vertical. The present lane isn’t completely flat but extends partially into the past and the present to accommodate vertical events (attention span). All alike-shaped events are stored in a mental construct, the event stack. The event stack is subject to constant change; it splits, merges, bends, and tilts. The flattened event stack produces the event mold, or envelope of an event. The event boundaries are fuzzy by nature; this fuzziness is one of the mind's biggest strengths and cradle of our creativity. The organism's impression of the present plane is not complete. Salience determines which events are actively observed (snatched). Change might be the most important aspect of salience: “what moves / occurs together belongs together.”

Co-occurrence is how the brain makes sense of the world. All meaning comes from context; that is, the observable circumstances that form the setting for an event. The event doesn’t occur in isolation; all secondary information of a specific event is stored in the stack bleed of an observed event. Events stored together are compaginated events. If an event is observed, the following occurs: the closest event stack is matched, and within the event tree the closest event pattern is fetched. Only n- i world points are observed in the present plane, the remaining i event patterns are completed when they are evoked from the recorded event. The present plane is patched, and perception is a hybrid state of the past and the present. The snatch-patch ratio corresponds to different mental states. Patching occurs on two different levels: modelling the real world (if you close your eyes, you'll still be able to walk across the room from memory), we call this virtual reality; and by providing context to the observed environment (if you observe a chair, you know what it's good for), we call this augmented reality. There are only two possible brain operations, and both rely on overlap of observed world points: jumping and rolling — 'this like that' and 'from / with this, that'. Jumping connects two event patterns from the same stack core by similarity (if you see a cow, you might be reminded of a horse; both fit the same pattern within given fuzziness limits). Rolling links two compaginated event patterns, a core event with a bleed event, which in turn becomes core event for the next thought (if you see a cow, you might think of milk, then of cereals, then of breakfast with family). We call this process thought-chain concatenation linking overlapping flakes of mink-space tissue. Observers become increasingly better at snatch, match, and patch through habit formation. Learning is intentional compagination and if we want to remember something, we pre-pair both events mentally. Habit formation strips a cloud band of concatenated event patterns of its bleed and reduces to its core components (pruning). Spatial perception is one result of routine formation; time intervals of dt → 0 are sufficient to determine relative positioning and movement.

The framework doesn’t distinguish between extra-organismic (ex-orgs) and intra-organismic world points (in-orgs). The organism detects and reacts to observed event patterns. An in-org pattern constitutes a bodily instruction set. The snatch-patch sequence limits the notion of free will as a given reaction is product of past event states and input for future states. The mink-space topology yields the organism's Lyapunov function. Free will is addressed in terms of malleability. If the organism is on its equilibrium worldline, malleability is high. That is, time concerns for match-and-patch are low and the organism can ponder future alternatives (consider high-complexity patches). Off the worldline, present concerns take over; feelings and emotions command the organism and steer it towards its attractor in free-energy terms. Intelligence, in its narrow definition, can be reduced to concatenation prowess and foresightedness in mink space. The framework speculates that different wavelengths might be associated with the temporal distance from the here-now; short-lived, high-frequency waves being related to time-critical (timelike) events like motion and low frequency waves to time-uncritical (spacelike) events like dreaming; classical thought (reasoning) lying in between. Time perception might have to do with here-now impressions; automated tasks (routines) are pre-timed and removed from active decision-making and the here-now, so is dreaming. Self-consciousness has been a by-product of an ever growing time horizon. Connotation contained in the stack bleed assigns meaning to an event; information contained in the Self stack assigns meaning to the organism itself. Self-awareness arises from stack depth and stack breadth and intertemporal comparison of event states. In certain mental states, insufficient regions of the fields are generated; the organism is unconscious. Every world state is characterized by a unique wave footprint; by regenerating this footprint, we create memory. As no two world states are identical, memory can never be perfect.

If we analyze what separates humans from other animals, one and only one faculty stands out: Humans were able to hijack the organism's propensity to react to external events. Man-made patterns made knowledge transferable and accumulable, thus expanding the field of consciousness to infinity. Inside the organism, information is stored in its integrated form, soft information; outside the organism, it is passed on in its isolated form, hard information. While soft information dies with its bearer, hard information lives on forever. But — soft information arises from hard information, and hard information on its own is worthless, unintelligible rubbish. Language is the first systematic approach to structure hard information; it's modeled closely after mink-space architecture: nouns resemble static patterns, verbs dynamic patterns, and sentences complex patterns. A delimited and defined pattern elevates its composing elements from inexpressible lower-level patterns (LLP) to top-level patterns (TLP). Snatched world patterns act as a wormhole that take the organism back to remote mink-space locations; while TLPs are traceable, LLPs act on the subconscious level. Naming converts formerly ineffable concepts like love and hate into traceable events.
