On the Origin of Mind

A Matter of Space and Time

1 Foreword 1.1 On the origin of this essay In this essay we want to approach the age-old question of mind and consciousness from a perspective of sensory space and, above all, time. It goes without saying that cannot offer a final answer but only new responses to old questions, and new questions to old answers. We cannot say how original or fruitful the presented explanation really is but we are somehow optimistic to provide a new perspective. The presented framework takes a very Bohmian approach to thinking. The foreword to his book Thought as a System reads: “Bohm points out that this fragmentary view corresponds to 'reality' in significant respects, but suggests that we have overextended our faith in the objectivist perspective. […] To begin with, thought is not fresh, direct perception. It is literally that which has been ‘thought’ — the past, carried forward into the present. It is the instantaneous display of memory, a superimposition of images onto the active, living present. On the one hand, this memory is what allows us to perform even the simplest of tasks, such as getting dressed in the morning. On the other hand, memory is also responsible for various aspects of fear, anxiety or apprehension, and the actions that proceed from these memories. Thought, then, is also inclusive of feelings, in the form of latent emotional experiences“. Semiotic gestalt principles stand at its center and we merge Jakob von Uexküll’s umwelt concept with Herman Minkowski four-dimensional space-time continuum. Like Merlin Donald with his Distributed Cognitive Networks or Andy Clark and David Chalmers with their notions of the Extended Mind we believe that the mind doesn’t end at its cranium. The model’s dynamic components fall in line with Edmund Husserl’s Theorem of Intentionality and Karl Friston’s Free Energy Principle and Bayesian view of Predictive Coding. Decision making must be seen in the tradition of Edward Thorndike’s 1898 Law of Effect and B.F. Skinner’s Operand Conditioning. Last but not least, we see the mind a product of co-evolution with its physical environment. This reconciles the present analysis with principles of Embodied Cognition and Emergent Properties. Drawing a steady but accelerating line beginning with the Big Bang, we can even link it to panpsychic ideas as old as Thales and Plato. We try to explain how things as complex as human intelligence could emerge on the basis of a few simple rules. We aspire to show how ‘glitches’ in the system (or, biases) could be useful to disclose its inner workings. The result is a framework that elegantly joins unrelated fields of investigation that concern the human species ranging from biology, psychology, sociology, or linguistics. The proposed mink-space framework has surprising explanatory power regarding subjects as diverse as thoughts and emotions, talent and intelligence, dreams and daydreams, personality and originality, learning and forgetting, self-awareness, language and culture, differences and commonalities between humans and animals, or free will. Throughout the presentation, we want to be as objective as possible; but to be honest all findings are based on very subjective observations and the last two paragraphs even descend into pure speculations. 2 Evolution 2.1 Definition of life Definition of life. The time horizon is an organism’s capacity to sense its changing environment. For the purpose of this framework we define life as any organized arrangement of matter that possesses a time horizon strictly greater than zero. Inanimate matter has a time horizon of zero. We declare an organism as dead when its field of consciousness, defined as the ambit of events an organism can observe, collapses and it decomposes into individual particles of time horizon zero. This very broad definition will allow us to draw a continuous line from primitive protocells to intelligent life. 2.2 Minkowski space Minkowski space adds time as a fourth dimension to three-dimensional Euclidian space. The here-now is the locus where the world line intersects the present plane that separates the past from the future. It constitutes the origin of spacetime for the observer. The world line is the path the observer traces through Minkowski space. The here-now is also the world point where the observers past and future light cone meet. Events within the light cone are timelike; that is, events in the past light cone could have an effect on the here-now and events in the future light cone could be affected by the here-now. We called the brain the organism’s sense of time in charge of detecting and inducing dynamic changes to its environment. A spacetime framework therefore seems to be promising starting point to better understand the origins of mind. Figure 1: Minkowski space. The observer is located at timespace origin located in the present plane. The present plane separates the absolute past from the absolute future. Light cones delimit timelike and spacelike regions. The timelike regions (inside the light cones) are regions where there can be a causal relationship between the event at the origin and an event within the timelike regions (where v < c). The spacelike regions (outside the light cones) are regions where there cannot be a causal relationship between the event at the origin and any event in the spacelike region (because v > c). For a given event E, the set of events that lie on or inside the past light cone of E would also be the set of all events that could send a signal that would have time to reach E and influence it in some way. Likewise, the set of events that lie on or inside the future light cone of E would also be the set of events that could receive a signal sent out from the position and time of E, so the future light cone contains all the events that could potentially be causally influenced by E. The observer’s worldline runs through the past and present light cone; it is the sequence of spacetime events corresponding to the history of the observer. 2.3 The field of consciousness Beyond eyesight organisms pick up on a wide range of different species-specific signals. Those signals travel at different velocities than light speed. We should therefore complement Minkowski space with additional axes that hold this information. Every organism is characterized by its own set of sensory inputs. We call this new continuum the Minkowski mind space, or Mink space in short. We denote the subset of conceivable world points the organism’s field of consciousness (FOC); it includes present, past and (suspected) future observations. Jakob von Uexküll explored in his 1909 book Umwelt und Innenwelt der Tiere the subjective perception of different species, and concluded that only mental idleness could have lead us to the misbelief that all animals share the same (our) objective world. The umwelt is highly subjective, and humans are unwittingly aware of that: If another organism doesn’t observe it, it doesn’t exist for it (why else our passion for sharing to cement our social standing). The umwelt theory states that the mind and the world are inseparable, because it is the mind that interprets the world for the organism. The umwelt comprises the organism’s body. Consequently, different umwelts differ for different organisms. The umwelt concept is hence the static version of the field of consciousness within this framework. Uexküll further distinguishes between merkwelt — the set of world points an organism can perceive — and wirkwelt — the set of world points an organism can effectively manipulate. These terms are related to the past and future mind cones in Mink space. Figure 2: The Black Ghost Knifefish. The Black Ghost Knifefish lives and hunts in the murky rivers of the Amazon River. Plant growth is driven by humidity in the soil and light in the sky hitting the organism’s surface. The development of sensory organs permitted the organism to penetrate its environment beyond its own frontier and to locate distant food sources in space. Rays emitted by the source can be picked up and traced back to it. Eyesight in particular proved to be an especially powerful tool; not only was its reach almost infinite reaching outwards to distant galaxies, it also offered a clear three-dimensional spatial perception. Eyesight equals foresight. The newly gained capabilities offered competitive advantages to newly emerging species: It facilitated organisms to navigate their environment, steer towards for food sources, to avoid obstacles and predators. Bodies grew and their territory expanded. As the understanding of space deepened, so did the understanding of time in classical co-evolution. Various dedicated organs had emerged to capture different signals reaching the organism from the environment. Simultaneously, we saw the development of the brain to coordinate those sensory inputs and the bodily responses. A developed sense of time was necessary to make sense of the plethora of sensory inflow. Many of those signals were received with varying delay; the bodily response had to be coordinated instructing different muscles at different times. Thus we could see the brain as the sensory organ of time. Distance and time are correlated. In a typical predator-prey scenario, time of contact increases with distance between them; so does the number of possible moves on both sides. Here, experience in the form of memory, timing, and — for the first time — strategy come into play. Malcolm McIver studied the Black Ghost Knifefish. These are nocturnal animals hunting in the murky river waters. They are a weakly electric fish which use an electric organ and receptors distributed over the length of their body in order to locate insect larvae. The electric field barely extends beyond body limits and due to its prolonged shape most of it covers the region to lateral to the direction of movement. This means it detects its prey usually when it has already swum half-way past it. Any action therefore is strongly reactive and involves little forward planning. From the example of the Black Ghost Knifefish on the other hand, it becomes immediately clear that a deeper sensory penetration of the environment and larger time horizon is not always necessary and could well turn out to be a waste of resources. The Bayesian probability for any world time to materialize, decreases exponentially with its distance from the here-now. For simple organisms it would simply be a capacitating overkill as most world points were located outside its field of operation. Evolutionary fitness is much more a matter of striking the right balance between needs and capabilities in order to be best adapted to the local environment. The species which eventually turned an extensive time horizon into a virtue was the human race. 2.6 Costs and benefits The human brain consumes up to 20% of the overall energy intake while only accounting for 2% of body mass; more than in any species on earth. Means must be found to compensate for those elevated energy needs: Just as predators followed their prey and predicted its next move, humans tracked the life cycle of crop and livestock over much longer periods of time. Tracking the life cycle of plants and livestock and extrapolating those observations offered the necessary calories to compensate for the brain’s elevated energy demand. It takes foresight and confidence that a certain event will materialize in the future to plant a seed in the here-now and harvest its fruits months later. Richard Wrangham argues that the advent of cooking improved the energy provision per meal even further. Pre-processing food outside the body breaks up tightly packed and inaccessible enzymes increasing energy density while reducing time and effort for chewing and digesting at the same time. Once again, studying causality and comparing the states of before and after — this time the effect of heat on food — gave the human race a critical edge. At one point, this reciprocal relationship in which energy offered foresight, and foresight offered energy, became self-reinforcing, letting the human race diverge from all other primates. Similarly, sleep seems to play a crucial role in developing and maintaining a healthy high-performance brain. During deep sleep the organism is especially vulnerable and exposed to a potentially hostile environment. Human ingenuity led to strong shelters that made continued and disconnected sleep possible. 2.7 Human advantage The ability to draw knowledge from past events, to detect causalities and make predictions about future events turned out to be nature’s sharpest weapon yet. A sense for chronological order proved to be an indispensable prerequisite to make sense of the world. Suddenly static snapshots turned into life and we could track their development. A series of disconnected world points gave rise to a differential analysis where different states could be compared and discrepancies among states could be discovered. Self-consciousness and compassion were an unplanned brainchild of this same development; from now on, a new-born was no longer just a new-born and remained a new-born in the eye of the observer. An old man was no longer an old man out of nowhere. When the first men began to study their fellow creatures, it was merely an exercise that enabled them to learn from foreign mistakes or to anticipate unknown situations. Soon later and fully unintentional, observation developed into empathy. Compassion proved to be a success strategy for society as a whole. In a similar way, observation lead to self-reflection; if that baby whose development I tracked, became a boy and then a man, then I too was a baby at one point in the past. The seed of self-awareness had just been planted. And for the first time, thoughts of decay and the cycle of life entered the realm of humanity. Intertemporal comparison of formerly isolated states thus constitutes the foundation of thought and personality. 3 Event patterns 3.1 Detect and react The organism stands in constant interaction with its environment; the mind’s role is to steers it successfully through its umwelt and thereby constructing the organism’s worldline. To fulfill its job, the brain must react appropriately to known situations avoiding past mistakes and make educated guesses about unknown situations. Mink space is a continuum of world points in which specific event points frequently reappear together. Those events form characteristic patterns, which the brains relies upon in order to make sense of its environment. Therefore, the mind must detect said event patterns, inspect them, elect the relevant ones, collect them in memory and correct existing memories if necessary, select the relevant memory and inject lessons learned into the present decision making process, and finally, react. We can summarize this process into three repetitive steps: snatch, match, and patch. Figure 3: Pattern detection. Pattern detection and delimitation is a critical skill for the organism to survive. Salience helps to separate an event pattern from its surroundings. Besides horizontal (static) salience, vertical (dynamic) salience plays a crucial role; that is, change along any mink space axis (motion, noise, smell, or temperature levels) — “what moves / appears together, belongs together”. 3.2 Snatching On its path through mink space, the organism’s sensory organs are exposed to a constant flow of inputs. The brain’s first task consists of detecting the relevant portions of this information stream and to delimit the event. Salience helps; being defined as the state or quality of standing out from its surroundings. Salience occurs along all sensory axes. A bee might pick up on colorful blossoms, a wolf on a scent trail on the ground, and a bird on the call of its peers. For a bird call to reach its fellow creature another bird must have exited its vocal cords first; for the wolf to note an odor change in its nostrils, wind must have carried it to him or the organism itself must have changed its position as the scent trail on the ground remains fixed. This brings us to the most important sensory axis of all — time. Above we stated that the brain is the sensory organ dedicated to time. The brain allows the organism to carry out intertemporal state comparisons. Without this property, the environment would be nothing more than frozen scenery. It would be hard or even impossible to discern any physical event clusters (objects). Motion against an unchanged background stands out — what moves together, belongs together. Moving pictures inevitably catch our attention, as anybody can tell by the effect of a TV screen in an empty bar. Changing relative positions to one another allows the brain to delimit event borders, understand space, and last but not least, time itself. Important though is not absolute change, but change relative to the observer’s frame of reference. An eagle might spot the moving rat from high above, but for the runner on the ground, a stone in her path or a branch in the air is the relevant object relative to her world line. Even the organism’s own sense of balance relies on relative changes and not on absolute motion. Figure 4: Pattern delimitation and refinement. The brain is tasked with making sense of the environment; that means separating the wheat from the chaff. Mink space is a continuous fabric and the brain must delimit the relevant portions that constitute an event pattern (‘gestalt’). Sometimes, limits are obvious in other cases hard to detect; salience simplifies the job. Unnecessary regions are trimmed (pruned), and omitted regions are added where needed. The Weber-Fechner law is based on the fact that senses react to relative changes in the environment. Nature is stuck in an eternal arms race ensuing from this circumstance; both prey and predator want go unnoticed by its adversary as long as possible. Camouflage minimizes visual salience; sneaking wild cats try to stay below the just-noticeable difference by reducing dynamic frame changes and the acoustic footprint. The opponent responds by favoring offspring with sharpened senses in future generations. 3.3 Gestalt and context Event patterns have two principal extensions: we denominate such developments along the time axis to be vertical and concurrent events in the present plane to be horizontal. World point topologies that have been evaluated to belong together are interpreted as whole (cf. gestalt theory). Thus, both the physical object ‘moon’ and the written expression are horizontal patterns that are snatched in one go. A similar phenomenon can be observed along the vertical axis: the spoken word ‘moon’ is observed as a single input too; an acoustic slice of dt → 0 doesn’t present a valuable data. Instead the entire string is interpreted as a whole. We could infer that the present plane isn’t completely flat, but offers a margin of tolerance accounting for latencies among different sensory inputs. Further, we can state that there is no information without context; isolated data world points don’t exist. 3.4 Compagination In a first rough approximation, all salient world points are stored together. We call this entanglement of world-point compagination. We can compare this process to a sculptor visiting the quarry and choosing the block of stone that catches his eyes. The sculptor already has a vague idea about the final shape of his endeavor. Like the artist, the brain isn’t unbiased in its choosing the first working portion either, but bases its choice in experience. As the sculptor moves around the crude block he begins isolating the shape from its surrounding and to add material where needed. This process involves subtracting from the crude shape (pruning) and adding material where needed. Through continued work all unnecessary portion are removed until only event-critical points are left. Likewise, additional event-critical world points are added in the process which initially escaped the sense. What occurs together belongs together. Within the mink-space framework we can distinguish three different patterns: static, dynamic, and complex patterns. All of them boil down to be world-point topologies in mink space. ‘Paul’ is a static pattern, ‘get closer’ is a dynamic pattern, and ‘meet Paul’ is a complex pattern. Figure 5: Present-plane depth. The present plane has a non-zero depth; its vertical limits are fuzzy and extend into past and future. This depth allows accommodating for some margin of error — not all sensory hit the observer’s here-now at the same time (e.g. light travels faster than sound). Present-plane depth translates colloquially into attention span. A non-zero attention span makes it possible to associate thunder with lightning, mold a spoken word into one single dynamic event pattern, or predict the ending of a sentence. Present-plane depth enables us to follow complex plots of a stage play extending over several hours. Co-occurrence is the way the brain makes sense of the world. Disperse event clouds along different axis are paired together during the compagination process. Thus, an event page represents an a priori unrelated, aleatory, and selective impression from the totality of world points within the observer’s FOC. It is important to note that horizontal events can be cross-sensory. From simultaneous or quasi-contemporaneous events the brain derives cohesiveness and causality: thunder follows lightning. While every species comes with a preadapted set of sensory skills and dedicated brain regions, missing signal feeds can be compensated by other senses. Through neo-sensory approaches, new axes which are foreign to the species can be added to its mink space and the brain learns to make sense of the provided sensory inflow. We learned that the mind entangles patterns that are located in mink-space proximity. Humans learned to exploit this fact by willfully presenting sensory information in the same present plane that consequently gets compaginated. Parents teach their children words by pointing animal pictures and repeating the animal’s name or sounds. This way one pattern gets paired with another. In a similar manner we instruct the mind to remember: to buy milk in the grocery store we pair the ‘milk’ pattern with the ‘supermarket’ pattern. 3.5 Matching The stack is a theoretical construct that houses all patterns of the same type in chronological order. Alike-shaped event topologies that appear scattered in mink space are vertically aligned along the time axis. By flattening the event stack and superimposing all event patterns we obtain the event mold or master pattern (stereotype). For the event mold the absolute mink space position is irrelevant; only the relative location of one world point to another matters. Certain event points in the event mold are more frequent and appear stronger just as some stars shine stronger inside a constellation. Therefore, memories aren’t carved in stone. The unfolded event stack resembles not so much a solid column but contrails in the sky. Individual world points in the flattened master pattern are subject to two forces: unused world points fade and are eventually disposed of (pruned); used world points are reinforced but can be reused in the process and compaginated in a new event pattern. A mind point shares the fate of an iceberg and will melt eventually. Two factors determine its lifespan: (a) size — strong initial accumulation, or continual increments, and (b) surroundings — more icebergs in its vicinity help it to stay fresh. This is one reason why procedural memories outlive declarative and episodic memories as cognitive functions deteriorate; in-org-world points are permanently within the organism’s FOC allowing a perpetual recharge; also, bodily movements are physically constrained limiting the extend of stack deformations. Declarative memories thin out and can lose context transforming from recollection to familiarity. Its boundaries become nebulous giving room to doubts and, making it susceptible to hearsay and false memories. These explanations are consistent with Paul Frankland’s suspicion that neurogenesis can complicate the challenge of retrieving prior memories from the hippocampus. Figure 6: The event mold. The event mold or master pattern is the flattened event stack. Super-positioning all individual event patterns produces the envelope for a given event. Within the envelope some regions will be reinforced over time while others degrade (because of stack bending). Stack tilting leads to growth of the envelope into one direction or another. The event mold is a living, wobbly entity itself and changes over time (not even accounting for event bleed); that is, the organism’s ‘idea’ of a certain thing is not carved in stone. 3.6 Fuzziness The superimposition of event patterns leads to fuzziness in the master image; its envelope is breathes like the reservoir of a bagpipe. We can compare the master to a music genre that can accommodate a wide range of songs that share some identifying features. It’s exactly this fuzziness that contributes most to the brain’s brilliant versatility. How powerful it really is becomes clear when comparing it to man-made alternatives: popular applications that help to identify songs based on their acoustic footprint struggle quickly when not presented with the original recording. The human mind on the other hand makes sense even from samples that are reproduced by other means (clapping, singing etc.). One can easily the shortcomings that a rigid system would entail: a one-to-one definition where one record matches one event would make no sense. The world flow doesn’t repeat itself. Nature of course found ways to exploit loopholes that are opened by the flexibility of the system. Both predators and prey make use of it by applying aggressive and defensive forms of mimicry. Fuzziness allows us to understand different versions of the same handwritten letter but it also allows fish in the ocean to take plastic for food — if it’s close enough, it’s good enough. 3.7 The event stack The sculptor’s work is a work in progress. The child that learned what a tiger looks like will see more than one depiction of the animal. The totality of tiger representations and viewing angles is stored in the event stack. New impressions are continuously added. Older ones move down the stack, get reinforced or fade. The ‘tiger’-event pattern constitutes the stack core. Every event impression was entangled with other secondary events that are compaginated on the stack entry. Those secondary events constitute the stack bleed. We can term the totality of bleed patterns the connotation of an event. The stack bleed makes any event unique to every observer as no to organisms share the same world line. Some bleed events are shared across a large number of the same of organisms within the same cohort as they share much of the same trajectory. Advertising companies frequently tap into this well of common experiences to evoke desired associations. Other bleed events are highly personal; oftentimes specific smells or noises are associated with specific places or activities for a concrete individual. These concurrent event patterns are generally speaking irrelevant drag-alongs but acquired significance for the corresponding observer. His brain booked the concomitant event pattern as event-critical side effect. Figure 7: The event stack. A visual representation of the event stack (stack core and horizontal bleed, not accounting for vertical bleed); the stack contains all alike-shaped event patterns vertically aligned and in chronological order. The stack contains all concurrent, compaginated events in the same time plane (connotation); it’s home to the ‘unconscious’, the iceberg below the surface. Stack and bleed are a living entity and subject to constant change. Figuratively speaking, we can highlight following four basic stack behaviors. Splitting occurs as the observer trains it senses and learns to distinguish and refine an increasing number of patterns. Teaching a child the concept of ‘fish’ you will find that she applies a rather lose standard and lumps seals and turtles together in the same category. The object must only be close enough in terms of shape and color. A few weeks later she will not only separate seals from fish, but also distinguish between fish, dolphins, and whales. A marine biologist might be capable of telling hundred different fish species apart and an adult wine experts will be able to differentiate more taste patterns than the average consumer. Stack merger occurs when a pattern loses importance for the organism and appears with less frequency in the observer’s FOC. A young teen might be able to distinguish dozens of dinosaurs or football players, and at a later stage these pattern braches will collapse to a single stack again. Stack tilting happens if the center of gravity of the event pattern shifts like a wandering dune. One example could be semantic shift of words; another one could the event-critical feature for ‘dad’ in the eyes of a child. A toddler might spot his father by the pair of shoes he wears just because his eyes are always on the ground as he learns to walk. Later on, this pattern feature will depreciated completely. Stack tinting is the filtering effect of newer pattern entries on older patterns down-stack. 3.8 Patching The mind helps the organism to make the best out of any given world state. It takes in the sensory input (snatch), looks up comparable past events (match and fetch), and finally complements the available information as best as it can (patch). Patching occurs on two completely different time scales. Senses can only focus on one world point at a time; at the same time the organism’s environmental perception is much more complete than this. The brain keeps just scanned world points readily available and populates the present plane with past event points. In this respect, the brain creates a virtual reality before the organism’s senses. The observer can switch off the light and still grasp the cup on the nightstand. The process resembles a video compression algorithm where only the altered segments of the screen are updated. The second dimension includes the provisioning of remote event patterns. In this capacity, the brain very much acts as augmented-reality device. When the observer encounters a face, food, or situation in general along the world line, the mind matches the stack and taps into it, fetches the best fit, and serves the immediately surrounding world-point cloud up to the here-now (patch). The information contained in the horizontal and vertical bleed of the remote event pattern contains valuable connotation (friend or foe, edible or toxic) and gives clues about what came before and what comes next. The letter A matches a known event type and can be complemented; the Vitruvian man follows symmetry laws and the missing half can be complemented; the depiction of modern art doesn’t follow any known matching rules and cannot be complemented. One event state triggers another event state in mink space by lighting up the shared world points. The new constellation in turn shares new overlapping experiences and so on. We can distinguish two fundamentally different manners how the brain blazes its trail through mink space: jumping and rolling. Jumping connects two distant event patterns by similarity (analogy). These are movements within the event stack and can be compared to functioning of an elevator that connects different floors of a building. The organism in the elevator has no idea about the distance covered and the route taken. Rolling is the path from one compaginated event pattern to another. It can be compared to the hallways within the building. Rolling can horizontally pair two frequently co-occurring events like ‘father’ and ‘son’; it can also pair vertically aligned event patterns like the flight trajectory of a football. The degree of overlap between patterns and the strength of individual world point strength favors the route of the cloud band through mink space. Higher charge attracts the passing thought chain to reconnect; think of two merging droplets that merging into one to reduce surface tension. Younger event points are — all other things equal — generally higher charged than older ones. That is, stack order matters. An agent might have a general preference for option A, but if option A failed in the last intent, she will be inclined to give option B a shot. Figure 9: Thought chain concatenation. Chains of thought find their way through the treasure trove of memories by two principle means: rolling and jumping. Rolling connects two previously compaginated events; theses can be vertically (sequence) or horizontally (concurrence) entangled. Jumping connects alike-shaped events (analogy). Former moves from one neighboring event in mink space two another, hence rolling; latter randomly lands on a similar event topology anywhere in mink space, hence jumping. The route chosen follows the rule of highest coincidence or overlap (relevance). 3.10 Memory retrieval Juan Linde-Domingo found in experiments that participants recalled more abstract information first — for example, whether the image was of an animal or a musical instrument. However, when a person sees a complex object for the first time, the brain initially records the small details, such as color schemes or patterns. The process of memory retrieval thus appears to be in stark contrast to the process of first-time image perception in the brain, according to him. His explanations fit neatly into the snatch-and-patch of the mink-space framework. The sensory organ scans the umwelt for key elements that contain hints which allow the brain to match the closest stack from where it finally retrieves the corresponding event pattern. The more details are scanned, the finer the returned image. Confronted with a new environment, the observer’s reality builds up incrementally even if the change seems instantly. At every step of the scan, the patching module serves up its best guess to the here-now to provide the organism with the best response possible for any given input. As the present plane becomes more detailed through sensory scans, the patching becomes more granular and refined. I want to share a few personal experiences to illustrate how this process unfolds in real life. The other day an unknown man approached me from behind, judging from the dynamic pattern and the neighborhood I was annoyed and continued. My first thought was: just another addict. As I turned around, I noticed that the person was older and more orderly than expected and I was inclined to help. My eyes continued to scrutinize the stranger and my ears listened to his words. A fraction of a second later, my sympathy changed from positive to negative once more. It became immediately clear that it wasn’t a helpless, old man in need but a bothersome missionary. On another occasion, I was strolling through the crowd. My eyes on the ground, I suddenly startled because I took a big black sports bag for a huge dog charging at me. The outline was constantly interrupted by the feet of other pedestrian on the street and other features like height, size, color and movement led my mind to a false conclusion that was revised only a second later. Other examples include misreading correctly spelled text or mentally auto-correctly spelled text, as well as misunderstanding words in a conversation. Thus, the umwelt is assembled before our eyes in a way that could be described as salience-weighted interlacing, in the progress top-level patterns are detected first before the mental image becomes ever more granular and higher-level patterns are integrated into the picture (pattern refinement). Adjacency can be horizontal (conjunction) — where there are palms there is water — and vertical (consequence) — thunder follows lightning. The other brain route is jumping (analogy); that is, the brain links alike-shaped event patterns — the Louvre reminds us of Egyptian pyramids. All thought follows these instructions. Analogy makes metaphors and parables such powerful linguistic tools; they map complex event patterns to a commonly known and understandable dynamic picture. Laying bricks is exhausting, so is thinking. Organisms prefer to avoid it. We prefer the well-known (faces, languages, ideologies etc.) and are pleased by symmetry. Successful latching is rewarded by the brain with a rush of happiness making the event more memorable (see emotions).
