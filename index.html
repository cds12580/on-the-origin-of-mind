On the Origin of Mind

A Matter of Senses, and Time

Preface

On the origin of OOOM.

In this essay I want to approach the age-old question of mind and consciousness from a perspective of sensory space and, above all, time. It goes without saying that no final answer will be presented but new responses to old questions, and new questions to old answers at best. I lost sight how original or fruitful the presented explanation really is but I am confident that every reader will find something new and inspiring.

The presented framework takes a very Bohmian approach to thinking. The foreword to David Bohm’s book Thought as a System reads: “Bohm points out that this fragmentary view corresponds to 'reality' in significant respects, but suggests that we have overextended our faith in the objectivist perspective. […] To begin with, thought is not fresh, direct perception. It is literally that which has been ‘thought’ — the past, carried forward into the present. It is the instantaneous display of memory, a superimposition of images onto the active, living present. On the one hand, this memory is what allows us to perform even the simplest of tasks, such as getting dressed in the morning. On the other hand, memory is also responsible for various aspects of fear, anxiety or apprehension, and the actions that proceed from these memories. Thought, then, is also inclusive of feelings, in the form of latent emotional experiences”. Helmholtzian unconscious inferences (German: unbewusster Schluss), also referred to as unconscious conclusion, play another important part. Their implications are further developed to account for dynamic processes like motion and decision making. Already around 1000 AD, Ibn al-Haytham used the word inference abundantly; in his Book of Optics (Arabic: كتاب المناظر) he wrote: “For that which sight perceives of a visible object by pure sensation is only the light and color in that object. As for the other properties that sight perceives of a visible object, such as shape, position, size, movement, and the like, these sights cannot perceive by pure sensation, but only by inference and signs. But neither [doctrine] is complete without the other, for sensation cannot be effected by virtue of one [of these two doctrines] without the other, nor can vision take place without their combination.” This framework builds upon Ibn al-Haytham's observations and applies them equally to all senses and extends their validity beyond measurable quantities.

Syllogisms are paired with semiotic gestalt principles and stand at the center of this analysis. We merge Jakob von Uexküll’s umwelt concept with Herman Minkowski’s four-dimensional space-time continuum. Like Merlin Donald with his Distributed Cognitive Networks or Andy Clark and David Chalmers with their notions of the Extended Mind, I believe that the mind doesn’t end at the cranium. The model’s dynamic components fall in line with Edmund Husserl’s Theorem of Intentionality and Karl Friston’s Free Energy Principle and Bayesian view of Predictive Coding. Decision making must be seen in the tradition of Edward Thorndike’s 1898 Law of Effect and B. F. Skinner’s Operand Conditioning. Last but not least, the mind is seen as a product of co-evolution with its physical environment. This reconciles the present study with principles of Embodied Cognition and Emergent Properties. Drawing a steady but accelerating line beginning with the Big Bang, we can even link it to panpsychic ideas as old as Thales and Plato.

The present approach tries to explain how things as complex as human intelligence could emerge on the basis of a few simple rules. I aspire to show how ‘glitches’ (biases) in the system could be useful to disclose its inner workings. The result is a framework that elegantly joins unrelated fields of investigation that concern the human species ranging from biology, psychology, sociology, or linguistics. The proposed mink-space concept has surprising explanatory power regarding subjects as diverse as thoughts and emotions, talent and intelligence, dreams and daydreams, personality and originality, learning and forgetting, self-awareness, language and culture, differences and commonalities between humans and animals, or free will.

The essay is about fifteen pages long and structured into nine sections — I Preface, II Evolution, III Event Patterns, IV The Organism, V The Super-Organism, VI Thought, VII Sleep, VIII The Brain, and IX Summary. Main arguments are made in sections III to VI; section VIII is there for the sake of completeness only and can be ignored. The whole work is written from a highly personal experience and must be read as such; it does not pretend to be scientific in any regard. I had to make up new terminology on the go; a short Glossary can be found on the next page.

Evolution

Definition of life.

The time horizon is an organism’s capacity to sense dynamic changes in its environment. For the purpose of this framework we define life as any organized arrangement of matter that possesses a time horizon strictly greater than zero. Inanimate matter has a time horizon of zero. We declare an organism as dead when its field of consciousness, defined as the ambit of events an organism can observe, collapses and it decomposes into individual particles of time horizon zero. This very broad definition will allow us to draw a continuous — but accelerating — line from primitive protocells to intelligent life.

Minkowski space.

Minkowski space adds time as a fourth dimension to three-dimensional Euclidian space. The here-now is the locus where the world line intersects the present plane that separates the past from the future. It constitutes the origin of spacetime for the observer. The world line is the path the observer traces through Minkowski space. The here-now is also the world point where the observers past and future light cone meet. Events within the light cone are timelike; that is, events in the past light cone could have an effect on the here-now and events in the future light cone could be affected by the here-now. I see the brain as the organism’s sense of time, in charge of detecting and inducing dynamic changes to its environment. A spacetime framework therefore seems to be promising starting point to better understand the origins of mind.

The field of consciousness.

Organisms pick up on a wide range of different species-specific signals. Those signals travel at velocities different than speed of light. We should therefore complement Minkowski space with additional axes that hold this information. Every organism is characterized by its own set of sensory inputs. We call this new continuum the Minkowski mind space, or Mink space in short. We denote the subset of conceivable world points the organism’s field of consciousness (FOC); it includes present, past and (suspected) future observations. Jakob von Uexküll explored in his 1909 book Umwelt und Innenwelt der Tiere the subjective perception of different species, and concluded that only mental idleness could have lead us to the misbelief that all animals share the same (our) objective world. The umwelt is highly subjective, and humans are unwittingly aware of that: If another organism doesn’t observe it, it doesn’t exist for it (why else our passion for sharing on social media to underpin our social standing). The umwelt theory states that the mind and the world are inseparable, because it is the mind that interprets the world for the organism. The umwelt comprises the organism’s body. Consequently, different umwelts differ for different organisms. The umwelt concept is therefore the static version of the field of consciousness advanced in this framework. Uexküll further distinguishes between merkwelt — the set of world points an organism can perceive — and wirkwelt — the set of world points an organism can effectively manipulate. These terms are related to the past and future mind cones in Mink space.

Space and the Sense of time.

Microorganisms only sense the most immediate environment around them and react to stimuli on their outer skin as they get in contact. Plant growth is driven by humidity in the soil and light in the sky hitting the organism’s surface. The development of sensory organs permitted the organism to penetrate the environment beyond its bodily limits and to locate distant food sources in three-dimensional space. Light rays, sound waves, or scent trails emitted by the source can be picked up and traced back to it. Eyesight in particular proved to be an especially powerful tool; not only was its reach almost infinite reaching outwards to distant galaxies, it also offered a clear three-dimensional spatial perception. Eyesight equaled foresight.

Those newly-gained capabilities proved to be valuable tools in a competitive world: It facilitated organisms to navigate their environment, steer towards for food sources, and avoid obstacles or predators. Organisms grew in size and roaming territory expanded. As the understanding of space deepened, so did the understanding of time in classical co-evolution. Various dedicated organs had emerged to capture different signals reaching the organism from the outside. Simultaneously, we saw the development of the brain to coordinate those sensory inputs and associated bodily responses. A developed sense of time was necessary to make sense of the plethora of sensory inflow — many of those signals were received with varying delay. The corporal response had to be coordinated too, instructing different muscles at different times. Without a doubt, we could name the brain the sensory organ of time.

Distance and time are closely linked. In a typical predator-prey scenario, time of contact increases as distance between them increases; and so does the number of possible moves on both sides. Choice and strategy were unheard of so far in the animal kingdom, but offered a huge advantage to whoever knew how to best exploit them. Experience in the form of memory was necessary to learn from past mistakes and improve one’s strategy. Experience from past events helped to extend trajectories into the future and develop foresight. Malcolm McIver studied the Black Ghost Knifefish. These are nocturnal animals hunting in the murky river waters of the Amazonas River. They are a weakly electric fish which use an electric organ and receptors distributed over the length of their body in order to locate insect larvae. The electric field barely extends beyond body limits and due to its prolonged shape most of it covers the region to lateral to the direction of movement. This means it detects its prey usually when it has already swum half-way past it. Any action therefore is strongly reactive and involves little forward planning. The Black Ghost Knifefish is a vivid example of what a near-zero time horizon looks like.

Striking the right balance.

One is tempted to conclude that more observable event states translate to higher chances of survival for the organism. From the example of the Black Ghost Knifefish, it becomes immediately clear that a deeper sensory penetration of the environment and larger time horizon isn’t always necessary and could well turn out to be a waste of resources. The Bayesian probability for any world point to materialize, decreases exponentially with its distance from the here-now. For simple organisms it would simply mean a capacitating overkill as most world points are located outside their field of operation. Evolutionary fitness is much more a about striking the right balance between needs and capabilities in order to be best adapted to the local environment. The species which eventually turned an extensive time horizon into a virtue was the human race.

Costs and benefits.

The human brain consumes up to 20% of overall energy intake while accounting for only 2% of body mass; more than any species on earth. Means must be found to compensate for those elevated energy needs: Just like a predator follows the trajectory of its prey and predicts its next move, humans tracked the life cycle of crop and livestock over much longer periods of time. Tracking the life cycle of plants and livestock and extrapolating those observations offered the necessary calories to compensate for the brain’s elevated energy demand. It takes foresight and confidence that a certain event will materialize in the future to plant a seed in the here-now and harvest its fruits months later. Richard Wrangham argues that the advent of cooking improved the energy provision per meal even further. Pre-processing food outside the body breaks up tightly packed and inaccessible enzymes increasing energy density while reducing time and effort for chewing and digesting at the same time. Once again, studying causality and comparing the states of before and after — this time the effect of heat on food — gave the human race a critical edge. At one point, this reciprocal relationship in which energy offered foresight, and foresight offered energy, became self-reinforcing, letting the human race diverge from all other primates. Similarly, sleep seems to play a crucial role in developing and maintaining a healthy high-performance brain. During deep sleep the organism is especially vulnerable and exposed to a potentially hostile environment. Human ingenuity led to strong shelters that made continued and disconnected sleep possible.

Human advantage.

The ability to draw knowledge from past events, to detect causalities and make predictions about future events turned out to be nature’s sharpest weapon yet. A sense for chronological order proved to be an indispensable prerequisite to make sense of the world. Suddenly static snapshots turned into life and we could track their development. A series of disconnected world points gave rise to a differential analysis where different states could be compared and discrepancies among states could be discovered. Self-consciousness and compassion were an unplanned brainchild of this same development; from now on, a new-born was no longer just a new-born and remained a new-born in the eye of the observer. An old man was no longer an old man out of nowhere. When the first men began to study their fellow creatures, it was merely an exercise that enabled them to learn from foreign mistakes or to anticipate unknown situations. Soon later and fully unintentional, observation developed into empathy. Compassion proved to be a success strategy for society as a whole. In a similar way, observation lead to self-reflection; if that baby whose development I tracked, became a boy and then a man, then I too was a baby at one point in the past. The seed of self-awareness had just been planted. And for the first time, thoughts of decay and the cycle of life entered the realm of humanity. Intertemporal comparison of formerly isolated states thus constitutes the foundation of thought and personality.

Event patterns

Detect and react.

The organism stands in constant interaction with its environment; the mind’s role is to steers it successfully through obstacles in mink space and thereby constructing the organism’s worldline. To fulfill its job, the brain must react appropriately to known situations avoiding past mistakes and make educated guesses about unknown situations. Mink space is a continuum of world points in which specific event points frequently reappear together. Those events form characteristic patterns, which the brains relies upon in order to make sense of its environment. Therefore, the mind must detect said event patterns, inspect them, elect the relevant ones, collect them in memory and correct existing memories if necessary, select the relevant memory and inject lessons learned into the present decision making process, and finally, react. We can summarize this process into three repetitive steps: snatch, match, and patch.

Snatching.

On its path through mink space, the organism’s sensory organs are exposed to a constant flow of inputs. The brain’s first task consists of detecting the relevant portions of this information stream and to delimit the event. Salience helps; being defined as the state or quality of standing out from its surroundings. Salience occurs along all sensory axes. A bee might pick up on colorful blossoms, a wolf on a scent trail on the ground, and a bird on the call of its peers. For a bird call to reach its fellow creature another bird must have exited its vocal cords first; for the wolf to note an change of odor in its nostrils, wind must have carried it to him or the organism itself must have changed its position as the scent trail on the ground remains fixed. This brings us to the most important sensory axis of all — time. Above we stated that the brain is the sensory organ dedicated to time. The brain allows the organism to carry out intertemporal state comparisons. Without this property, the environment would be nothing more than frozen scenery. It would be hard or even impossible to discern any physical event clusters (objects). Motion against an unchanged background stands out — what moves together, belongs together. Moving pictures inevitably catch our attention, as anybody can tell by the effect of a TV screen in an empty bar, or a tablet in the hands of a toddler. Changing relative positions to one another allows the brain to delimit event borders, understand space, and last but not least, time itself. Important though is not absolute change, but change relative to the observer’s frame of reference. An eagle might spot the moving rat from high above, but for the runner on the ground, a stone in her path or a branch in the air is the relevant object relative to her world line. Even the organism’s own sense of balance relies on relative changes and not on absolute motion.

The Weber-Fechner law is based on the fact that senses react to relative changes in the environment. Nature is stuck in an eternal arms race ensuing from this circumstance; both prey and predator want go unnoticed by its adversary as long as possible. Camouflage minimizes visual salience; sneaking wild cats try to stay below the just-noticeable difference by reducing dynamic frame changes and the acoustic footprint. The opponent responds by favoring offspring with sharpened senses in future generations. Spatial perception is the product of previous records of relative position changes; either the observer relative to another fixed or moving objects or two observed objects relative to one another. This rolling operation (see below) is later mentally unrolled and applied to alike-shaped event patterns (see jumping below). A trained ear might distinguish a song from a few tones; those fractions of a second are sufficient to be indicative of the entire song pattern. We are all experts at distinguishing spatial patterns; infinitesimal small changes in angle dф → 0 or distance dd → 0 are enough to discern relative positioning.

Gestalt and context.

Event patterns have two principal extensions: we denominate such developments along the time axis to be vertical and concurrent events in the present plane to be horizontal. World point topologies that have been evaluated to belong together are interpreted as whole (cf. gestalt theory). Thus, both the physical object ‘moon’ and the written expression are horizontal patterns that are snatched in one go. A similar phenomenon can be observed along the vertical axis: the spoken word ‘moon’ is observed as a single input too; an acoustic slice of dt → 0 doesn’t present a valuable data. Instead the entire string is interpreted as a whole. We could infer that the present plane isn’t completely flat, but offers a margin of tolerance accounting for latencies among different sensory inputs. Beyond salience, our attention is directed to what we know. I don’t usually pay attention to license plates while walking the streets — at least not consciously. But if I find the license plate of my home town in an unexpected location it frequently catches my eye (what is the likelihood for that?). Further, we can state that there is no information without context; no data world-point occurs in isolation.

Compagination.

In a first rough approximation, all salient world points are stored together. We call this entanglement of world-points compagination. In above figure, several top-level events are compaginated in the same horizontal plane. But compagination is not limited to concurrent event patterns only; vertical compagination captures the unfolding of dynamic event threads. We can compare this process to a sculptor visiting the quarry and choosing the block of stone that catches his eyes. The sculptor already has a vague idea about the final shape of his endeavor. Like the artist, the brain isn’t unbiased in its choosing the first working portion either, but bases its choice on experience. As the sculptor moves around the crude block he begins isolating the shape from its surrounding and to add material where needed. This process involves subtracting from the crude shape (pruning) and adding material where needed. Through continued work all unnecessary portion are removed until only event-critical points are left. Likewise, additional event-critical world points are added in the process which initially escaped the sense. What occurs together belongs together.

Within the mink-space framework we can distinguish three different patterns: static, dynamic, and complex patterns. All of them boil down to be world-point topologies in mink space. ‘Paul’ is a static pattern, ‘get closer’ is a dynamic pattern, and ‘meet Paul’ is a complex pattern. Different complex patterns can be further refined and evoke different fine-tuned responses. E.g., 'meet elder' will be followed by different salutes when additional umwelt patterns are taken into account: 'woman' or 'man', 'friend' or 'stranger', 'at home' or 'in public', 'in a Western country', 'in Asia' or 'in a Middle Eastern country' etc.

Co-occurrence is the way the brain makes sense of the world. Disperse event clouds along different axis are paired together during the compagination process. Thus, an event page represents an a priori unrelated, aleatory, and selective impression from the totality of world points within the observer’s FOC. It is important to note that horizontal events can be cross-sensory. From simultaneous or quasi-contemporaneous events the brain derives cohesiveness and causality: thunder follows lightning. While every species comes with a preadapted set of sensory skills and dedicated brain regions, missing signal feeds can be compensated by other senses. Through neo-sensory approaches, new axes which are foreign to the species can be added to its mink space and the brain will learn to make sense of the provided sensory inflow.

Quae simult sunt a phantasia comprehensa, si alterutrum occurat, solet secum alterum representare. — Juan Luis Vives, 1538

We learned that the mind entangles patterns that are located in mink-space proximity. Humans learned to exploit this fact by willfully presenting sensory information in the same present plane that consequently gets compaginated. Parents teach their children words by pointing animal pictures and repeating the animal’s name or sounds. This way one pattern gets paired with another. In a similar manner we instruct the mind to remember: to buy milk in the grocery store we pre-pair the ‘milk’ pattern with the ‘supermarket’ pattern mentally.

Matching.

The sculptor’s work is a work in progress. The child that learned what a tiger looks like will see more than one depiction of the animal. The totality of tiger representations and viewing angles is stored in the event stack. We call the process of associating an event pattern ‘in the wild’ with its mental representation matching. New impressions are continuously added. Older ones move down the stack, get reinforced or fade. The ‘tiger’-event pattern constitutes the stack core. Every event impression was entangled with other secondary events that are compaginated on the stack entry. Those secondary events constitute the stack bleed. We can term the totality of bleed patterns the connotation of an event. The stack bleed makes any event unique to every observer as no to organisms share the same world line. Some bleed events are shared widely across a large number of organisms within the same cohort as they share much of the same trajectory. Advertising companies frequently tap into this well of common experiences to evoke desired associations. Other bleed events are highly personal; oftentimes specific smells or noises are associated with specific places or activities for a concrete individual. These concurrent event patterns are generally speaking irrelevant drag-alongs but acquired significance for the corresponding observer because his brain determined the concomitant event as event-critical.

The event stack.

Even a delimited pattern on itself means nothing. In everyday life there are little instances when a presented event is completely alien to us because our brain does such an incredibly good job at identifying them and guiding our focus to known. Even foreign scripture is not completely alien to us; we recognize it as such or as human artefacts at the least. The only available information to ascribe to an unknown object is its mink-space environment. Already Ibn al-Haytham noted in his Book of Optics, that the surroundings provide references about measurable characteristics like size, distance or speed. We extend this notion to all the event's properties, quantitative and qualitative; all meaning come from the environment. This information adds up over time, forming the pattern's event stack: Through its event stack (see soft information below) a real-world pattern (hard information) gains meaning. The organism itself is an event pattern too; its connotation is stored in the Self stack. The Self stack must be nourished to gain meaning just like any other object; only if there is sufficient secondary information attached to an event it can be described. A newborn's self-awareness is limited to immediate impressions; its Self stack needs to gain depth and breadth over time in order to form an identity.

Stack and bleed are a living entity and subject to constant change. Figuratively speaking, we can highlight following five basic stack behaviors. Splitting occurs as the observer trains it senses and learns to distinguish and refine an increasing number of patterns. Teaching a child the concept of ‘fish’ you will find that she applies a rather lose standard and lumps seals and turtles together in the same category. The object must only be close enough in terms of shape and color. A few weeks later she will not only separate seals from fish, but also distinguish between fish, dolphins, and whales. A marine biologist might be capable of telling hundred different fish species apart and an adult wine experts will be able to differentiate more taste patterns than the average consumer. Stack merger occurs when a pattern loses importance for the organism and appears with less frequency in the observer’s FOC. A young teen might be able to distinguish dozens of dinosaurs or football players, and at a later stage these pattern braches will collapse to a single stack again. Stack tilting happens if the center of gravity of the event pattern shifts like a wandering dune. One example could be semantic shift of words; another one could the event-critical feature for ‘dad’ in the eyes of a child. A toddler might spot his father by the pair of shoes he wears just because his eyes are always on the ground as he learns to walk. Later on, this pattern feature will depreciated completely. Bending occurs if the event footprint remains the same but one region is frequently stressed more than another. Stack tinting is the filtering effect of newer pattern entries on older patterns down-stack.

The event mold.

The event stack is a theoretical construct that houses all patterns of the same type in chronological order. Alike-shaped event topologies that appear scattered in mink space are vertically aligned along the time axis. By flattening the event stack and superimposing all event patterns we obtain the event mold or master pattern (stereotype). For the event mold the absolute mink space position is irrelevant; only the relative location of one world point to another matters. Certain event points in the event mold are more frequent and reinforced. Therefore, memories aren’t carved in stone. The event stack resembles not so much a solid column but the tail of comet. Mold points are subject to two forces: unused world points fade and are eventually disposed of (pruned); other world points are reinforced but reused in the process and compaginated in a subsequent event patterns. A mind point shares the fate of an iceberg and will melt eventually. Two factors favor its lifespan: size — larger is better — and surroundings — more icebergs in its vicinity help to stay fresh. This is one reason why procedural memories outlive declarative and episodic memories as cognitive functions deteriorate; in-org-world points stay permanently in the organism’s FOC allowing a perpetual recharge; also, bodily movements are physically constrained limiting the extend of stack deformations. Declarative memories thin out and can lose context diminishing from recollection to familiarity. Its boundaries become frayed giving room to doubts and making it susceptible to hearsay and false memories. These explanations are consistent with Paul Frankland’s suspicion that neurogenesis can complicate the challenge of retrieving prior memories from the hippocampus.

Fuzziness.

The superimposition of event patterns leads to fuzziness in the master image; its envelope is like a breathing bagpipe. We can compare the master pattern to a music genre that can accommodate a wide range of songs that share some unifying features. It’s exactly this fuzziness that contributes most to the brain’s brilliant versatility. How powerful it really is becomes clear when comparing it to man-made alternatives: popular applications that help to identify songs based on their acoustic footprint struggle quickly when not presented with the original recording. The human mind on the other hand makes sense even from samples that are reproduced by other means (clapping, singing etc. ). One can easily the shortcomings that a rigid system would entail: a one-to-one definition where one record matches one event would make no sense at all. The world flow doesn’t repeat itself. Nature of course found ways to exploit loopholes stemming from the flexibility of the system. Both predators and prey make use of it by applying aggressive and defensive forms of mimicry. Fuzziness allows us to understand different versions of the same handwritten letter but it also is the reason why fish in the ocean take plastic scrap for food — if it’s close enough, it’s good enough.

Patching.

The mind helps the organism to make the best out of any given world state. It takes in the sensory input (snatch), looks up comparable past events (match and fetch), and finally complements the available information as best as it can (patch). Patching occurs on two completely different time scales. Senses can only focus on one world point at a time; at the same time the organism’s environmental perception is much more complete than this. The brain keeps recently scanned world points readily available and populates the present plane with them. In this respect, the brain creates a virtual reality before the observer: You can switch off the light and still grasp the cup on the nightstand. The process resembles a video compression algorithm where only the altered segments of the screen are updated. The second dimension includes the provisioning of remote event patterns. In this capacity, the brain very much acts as augmented-reality device. When the observer encounters a face, food, or situation in general along the world line, the mind matches the stack and taps into it, fetches the best fit, and serves the immediately surrounding world-point cloud up to the here-now (patch). The information contained in the horizontal and vertical bleed of the remote event pattern contains valuable connotation (friend or foe, edible or toxic) and gives clues about what came before and what comes next.

Ibn al-Haytham said it better than I when he wrote, “It is only by recognition that sight perceives what a visible object is. And recognition is not perception by pure sensation, since sight does not recognize all that it has seen earlier. […] If recognition without remembering is not possible, then recognition is not perception by pure sensation, but rather it is perception through a kind of inference. […] Now recognition may be of an individual object or of a species. Recognition of an individual object occurs as a result of likening the form of the visible object which the sight perceives at the time of recognition to the form it has formerly perceived of it. Recognition of a species occurs as a result of likening the form of the visible object to that of similar individuals of the same species which the sight has formerly perceived.” About the speed of this patching process he said, “Moreover, perception of many of the objects of vision that are perceived by judgement and inference occurs in an extremely short interval of time, and in many cases it is not manifest that perception of them occurs by means of judgement and inference because of the speed of the inference through which those objects are perceived and the speed of their perception by inference. Most of the objects of vision that are perceptible by inference are, therefore, perceived extremely quickly. And because of the speed of this perception it does not become apparent in most cases that they are perceived by inference and discernment; and they are perceived quickly because their premises are manifest and the faculty of judgement has become well accustomed to discerning them.”

Jumping and rolling.

The thought chain through the brain is governed by the rule of highest overlap. One event state triggers another event state in mink space by lighting up the shared world points. The new constellation in turn shares new overlapping experiences and so on. We can distinguish two fundamentally different manners how the brain blazes its trail through mink space: jumping and rolling. Jumping connects two distant event patterns by similarity (analogy). These are movements within the event stack and can be compared to functioning of an elevator that connects different floors of a building. The organism in the elevator has no idea about the distance covered and the route taken. Rolling is the path from one compaginated event pattern to another (vicinity). It can be compared to the hallways within the building. Rolling can horizontally pair two frequently co-occurring events like ‘father’ and ‘son’; it can also pair vertically aligned event patterns like the flight trajectory of a football. The degree of overlap between patterns and the strength of individual world point strength favors the route of the cloud band through mink space. Higher charge attracts the passing thought chain to reconnect; think of two merging droplets that merging into one to reduce surface tension. Younger event points are — all other things equal — generally higher charged than older ones. That is, stack order matters. An agent might have a general preference for option A, but if option A failed in the last intent, she will be inclined to give option B a shot.

Memory retrieval.

Juan Linde-Domingo found in experiments that participants recalled more abstract information first — for example, whether the image was of an animal or a musical instrument. However, when a person sees a complex object for the first time, the brain initially records the small details, such as color schemes or patterns. The process of memory retrieval thus appears to be in stark contrast to the process of first-time image perception in the brain, according to him. His explanations fit neatly into the snatch-and-patch of the mink-space framework. The sensory organ scans the umwelt for key elements that contain hints which allow the brain to match the closest stack from where it finally retrieves the corresponding event pattern. The more details are scanned, the finer the returned image. Confronted with a new environment, the observer’s reality builds up incrementally even if the change seems instantly. At every step of the scan, the patching module serves up its best guess to the here-now to provide the organism with the best response possible for any given input. As the present plane becomes more detailed through sensory scans, the patching becomes more granular and refined. I want to share a few personal experiences to illustrate how this process unfolds in real life. The other day an unknown man approached me from behind, judging from the dynamic pattern and the neighborhood I was annoyed and continued. My first thought was ‘just another addict’. As I turned around, I noticed that the person was older and more orderly dressed than expected and I was inclined to help. My eyes continued to scrutinize the stranger and my ears listened to his words. A fraction of a second later, my sympathy changed from positive to negative once more. It became immediately clear that it wasn’t a helpless, old man in need but a bothersome missionary. On another occasion, I was strolling through the crowd. My eyes on the ground, I suddenly startled because I took a big black sports bag for a huge dog charging at me. The outline was constantly interrupted by the feet of other pedestrian on the street; at the same time, features like height, size, color and movement matched and led my mind to a false conclusion that was revised only a second later. Other examples include misreading correctly spelled text, mentally auto-correcting misspelled text, or misunderstanding words in a conversation. Thus, the umwelt is assembled before our eyes in a way that could be described as salience-weighted interlacing, in the progress bottom-stack patterns are detected first before the mental image becomes ever more granular and higher-level patterns are integrated into the picture (pattern refinement).

The organism

Movement.

We distinguish between intra-organismic world points (in-orgs) belonging to the body of the observer and extra-organismic world points (ex-orgs) being the remaining mink space. Already Jakob von Uexküll’s umwelt concept explicitly included the observer’s body. In fact, the body is just an agglomeration of observable and traceable world points. The main difference being that these world points are under the control of the organism itself. Nonetheless, basic co-occurrences must be learned by the mind. The day the organism sees the light of day basic instruction sets are already deeply grounded. These chains are at the bottom of the stack, deeply carved-in patterns with low malleability. They are the tree trunk future instructions build on. By instructing in-orgs, the organism creates its world path through mink space. Movement is not a given and no other animal takes longer to gain full control over its internal world points than the human race. By trial and error, the brain pairs cause and effect. Different instructions lead to different vertical outcomes; many of them undesired. Every step is a controlled fall. While reaction R was found accidentally in the first place action A — leading to reaction R — can later be evoked intentionally when reaction R is the desired outcome, even if it was a priori impossible to predict that action A was the sought after cause. Successful pairings are reinforced and unsuccessful pairings discarded. Once the right outcome is hit, success is rewarded. This process is sometimes called shaping. By controlling its body, the organism controls its environment. This line of thought integrates smoothly with Edmund Husserl’s theorem of intentionality, Karl Friston’s free energy principle, and Andy Clark’s concept of predictive processing.

Time keeping.

Motion translates essentially into vertical event patterns in mink space; it is a sequence of event states. For a changing position, its time derivative is its velocity and its second derivative is its acceleration. Also, many other fundamental quantities in science are also time derivatives of one another: force is the time derivative of momentum, power is the time derivative of energy, and electric current is the time derivative of electric charge. Beyond doubt, time is crucial for movement. We must ask ourselves how the organism measures time. In our daily life we need repetitive phenomena in the universe to measure the passage of time. The earth for example, revolves around the sun and rotates around its own axis. It does so in a predictable fashion and both patterns are comparable to one another. Mankind used this fact by observing the sky and inventing sundials. Later we discovered that we could use harmonic oscillators in pendulum clocks and oscillating properties of quartz crystals for timekeeping. All behave in the same predictable way. It seems reasonable to assume that the organism relies on reliable, repeating patterns in a similar fashion. Standing waves are a suited first candidate. Absolute time keeping seems to be necessary for timing of actions. To construct a timeline, relative time keeping is used by the observer. Relative time keeping is achieved by comparing mink space locations and concatenation of observed event patterns.

Time perception.

Time perception is subjective and a combination of both absolute and relative time measurement. The umwelt is constructed of snatched world points (SWP) and patched world points (PWP); former are the product of snatching, the latter the product of patching. Perceived time duration depends on the SWP/PWP ratio per unit of time compared to an average reference rate (RR). During episodes of high alert, all attention is directed to the here-now and otherwise irrelevant details are registered. This augments the number of decisions for a fixed period of time. The higher than usual number of imprints stretches the relative component of time. Unknown environments, danger, curiosity, and other factors all contribute to higher alert states. This explains why we experience years in our youth longer than at older age, the outbound trip longer than the inbound leg, and why we have vivid and detailed memories of moments when we feared for our lives. Only here-now impressions count towards perceived time; habits and sleep (see respective sections below) are two instances for which this criterion doesn’t apply. This line of argument is compatible with theories presented by David Eagleman & Vani Pariyadath, and Giorgio Marchetti. States of high alert are closely linked to the next subject in our investigation: emotions.

Emotions.

The terms emotions, feelings, bodily needs and desires are treated interchangeably in this document due to lack of better knowledge; all refer loosely speaking to a cocktail of chemical in-org reactions. If the organism’s purpose is to successfully navigate the imponderable vastness of mink space, then emotions are the evolutionary protocol to keep it on course. They steer the observer towards beneficial encounters and away from detrimental ones. A specific umwelt pattern or a vague umwelt topology provokes a patching reaction that consists of a certain in-org event (the 'chemical cocktail'). Usually this reaction is genetically encoded to the organism but it can include learned behaviors too. In latter case, the patch reaction is contained in the event's stack bleed. The patch itself serves as input pattern for a subsequent reaction — do that, do not do that. If the whole process becomes misaligned through habit formation (see below) or runs out of hand entirely, we speak of compulsions and addictions. I for example, cannot resist the temptations of a cold beer. While they don’t taste as palatable as they used to and my head hurts much quicker than it used to, they are inevitably linked to those blissful sensations in my youth with friends after school. When having a coffee in a bar, I habitually pull the newspaper from the table next to me closer and start reading. It used to be satisfying to absorb all the news; today it's just more of the same and I lay it aside quickly. Nonetheless, old desires don’t die.

Stress and insomnia indicate being behind the planned trajectory, fear an unavoidable and undesirable collision, and anger indicates a missed the goal. Sadness and frustration may imply the awareness of being unable to attain a certain desirable goal. Anger and frustration shall prevent the organism from repeating the same mistakes. Therefore, worries are the price of progress, and to care a precondition for change. Joy and excitement on the other hand compensate for being on track or having met a target. Surprise and anticipation reflect unexpected and expected developments and help to adjust future forecasts. Fun is a learning strategy in an uncritical environment where a projected trajectory takes an unforeseen (comical) turn. A toddler may laugh when his parent disappears or emerges behind a chair. The same experience can proof critical when expecting life-threatening animals in high grass. Hunger and saturation regulate energy intake. Laziness favors procrastination where effort seems unnecessary and resources can be saved. Love creates advantageous bonds and hate creates distance where appropriate. Trust and distrust bind or repel the organism from other organism based on past experiences. Proudness and shame mediate social coherence. Hunger and thirst drive it towards food and water; satiety keeps it away.

Event tracking.

Emotions act on two main channels. Firstly, they strongly shift the scope of the time horizon towards the here-now. Humans become more animal-like. The present moment takes over future concerns. It is hard to concentrate on elaborate thoughts when being hungry or angry. Any thought will be drawn inbound limiting the attention focus to the immediate world line. The second main function of emotions concerns memory formation and retrieval. Under usual circumstances memories are reinforced by repetition and the habitual attention span is very short — out of FOC, out of mind. In the case of emotions, an ex-org pattern gets pegged to in-org pattern through compagination. In a first step, a given ex-org world pattern evokes the evolutionary in-org pattern ‘emotion’. Next, the observed ex-org event pattern moves out FOC; while the chemical cocktail reverberates for a prolonged period of time. Finally, the ‘emotion’ pattern evokes the past world pattern bringing it back to focus. The cycle repeats until the chemical cocktail is consumed. Virtual repetition guarantees a higher probability for a life-critical event to be burnt into memory. Future life events that evoke the same emotions will retrieve the initial event and its lessons stored in the event bleed. The mood-congruent memory bias describes behavior that supports this surmise.

Any event can be tracked by the observer on its journey through mink space. Emotions tie them to the observer’s mind, keeping them in FOC. They are the mechanism by which active threads are hooked to the mind and completed threads unhooked. The Zeigarnik effect points into a similar direction. Possible perils arise with excessive media consumption and social-media news feeds since threads never escape the observation foreground leading to a tracking overload. A third channel emotions affect the observer, is by nourishing social coherence through compassion. The German idiom ‘sich in jemanden hineinversetzen’, means placing oneself into someone else’s body. It thus explicitly includes the in-org world points of the other organism. Mirroring facial expressions of a peer helps the observer to sympathize. The ‘facial expression’ pattern is patched with the ‘emotion’ event pattern associate with it. Reliving peer emotions fosters empathy and cooperation among group members.

The Subconscious.

Intuition is located half way between emotional instincts and thought. Thought is characterized by a clearly delimited path through mink space; intuition includes large parts of the connotation contained in the bleed of a single thought-chain event widening the catchment area. It’s a trade-off between specificity and accuracy; if thought is a sniper rifle, then intuition is a shotgun, and emotions a mine field. Every thought chain excites its bleed field too. Much of what is commonly referred to as sub-consciousness is contained in the event bleed as secondary input. A clear-cut top-level pattern (TLP) sets off a delimited thought chain; numerous lower-level patterns (LLP) on the other hand trigger a broad response and can usually not be described in simple terms. A famous and illustrative example would be chicken sexing; chicken sexers can manually sort poultry at a speed of 8,000 chicks per day and 99. 7 percent accuracy by learning to identify the external appearance of the birds’ sexual organs that are located within their bodies. Taste — both in its gastronomical and esthetical sense of the word — would be another example. It is very hard to acquire and describe ‘good taste’, and childhood food preferences are deeply engrained. The place where a person is born and raised significantly influences his eating and music-listening habits. Connotations from experiences early in life are plenty and acquired under increased sensitivity (many life-firsts, thus more salience). Sub-consciousness resides in the hive of LLP events that is the stack bleed. These lower-order reactions are weak but manifold. Emotions that are released in these mini-patches amount to the so-called gut feeling. Mindless humming of a person oftentimes exposes the psychological state of the actor. Song content and a certain emotion have been entangled in the past; now the umwelt evokes the same feeling, and therefore the same song. The time axis and the inner clock can constitute an important sub-conscious agent too. An earworm is self-reinforcing in this respect; the song’s rhythm gets paired to the organism’s time-keeping mechanism. Once the song ends the body clock keeps ticking and can re-evoke the ceased melody.

Free will.

For many situations, decision making boils down to intertemporal trade-offs. No other species beside the human race is able to imagine world points as distant as the big bang in the past and the end of the universe in the future. No other species is presented with such a large number of choices. As long as the observer’s world line coincides with the equilibrium trajectory, the organism is free of needs. Deviations compel the organism to take actions through emotions. In critical situations the present moment always gains the upper hand against future planning — there’s no tomorrow without today. Relate phenomena are often referred to as ego depletion. The same way in-org-event patterns draw the time horizon to the here-now, any ex-org-event pattern causes a snatch-and-patch reaction. Vastness of observable world points makes any world state practically unpredictable; but the more general rule of detect-and-react leaves little room for free will anyhow.

Conditioning.

Classical conditioning tricks the brain into turning an objectively neutral event to be interpreted as causal factor for an innate response. Operational conditioning pairs an in-org pattern with a subsequent event. Edward Thorndike found that learning is product of accidental trial and error and formulated the Law of Effect. Such event patterns that precede desired outcomes are eventually reinforced and others discarded. Once again, the brain’s desperate effort for pattern detection and search for causality can lead the mind to seeing relationship between event patterns where no such connections exist. B. F. Skinner has shown such behavior by converting pigeons into superstitious creatures. Ultimately, humans don’t seem to be the only organisms that see themselves as the center of the universe; the umwelt filters the environment through the organism’s unique perception and overstate its own importance. The search for causality and the presence of superstition runs deep through human history, but it most likely precedes the emergence of the human race. Humans were always inventive in finding explanations for unresolved causalities; religion, cults, and pagan customs are riddled with them: Plagues and crop failures were blamed on God's wrath for misdemeanors in the community; millenarian cargo cults believed that ritualistic acts such as the building of an airplane runway will result in the appearance of material wealth.

Malleability.

We can define malleability as a weaker form of free will. If the organism is aware that the environment determines its decisions, it can choose its environment. Nonetheless, we must point out that the decision was taken by the same organism that wants to change. Malleability is greatly determined by stack depth which in turn correlates with age of acquisition. Heart beat for example is completely outside the observer’s control. Motoric skills, hearing, or pronunciation are acquired at early age and increasingly arduous to change later in life; non-native speakers have difficulties to distinguish and imitate fine acoustic subtleties of a foreign language. Ideas adopted later in life are more prone to be altered. Down-stack patterns have been tried and tested over ages of evolution; thoughts mark the vanguard of development where everything is still trial and error.

Along its own worldline the organism’s malleability is limited by its time horizon and narrow down quickly as they get closer to the here-now. Humans have the largest time horizon by far; they can imagine a state where they want to see themselves in the future but they cannot change overnight. In the here-now, every umwelt state inevitably leads to the next; malleability in the mink-space origin is zero. For the organism to behave as it intends to, he must change its umwelt. A clear example is the state of drunkenness. Humans’ foresight is wide enough to anticipate its consequences. The agent is aware of mischief and hangover. Once he has chosen to go this path it’s too late to take control; the here-now acts like rapids in the course of the stream of time. Mink space densifies and freedom of action is zero. The choice of whether to drink or not tomorrow must not be mistaken for freedom of will as it is completely determined by current umwelt state: is it a meeting with friends or a business dinner; was the last drinking fun or ended with severe headaches? The probability to reach any location in mink space can be illustrated looking at the Galton board. If we hold the ball fix and move the board below, it becomes increasingly more unlikely for any spot on the board to hit the ball the farther the spot is located from the world line.

Habits.

The here-now constitutes a bottle neck in mink space for the organism. We could go as far to postulate that only one instruction can take center stage at mink-space origin at a time, if we exclude quasi-contemporary processes. Habits (or routines) are the organism’s strategy to overcome this restriction. The organism learns and practices through compaginating consecutive event patterns by trial and error, or by observing another organism undergoing this process. We refer to learning if it only affects ex-org world points, and practice if in-org world points are involved too. Trial and error implies to travel many different cloud chains in mink space and repeated sub-assessments at crossroads. Through repetition the observer cuts back on unnecessary detours, optimizing the trajectory. Figuratively speaking, habit creation means stack reduction; non-essential event patterns are purged (pruned) and stack bleed is strongly reduced or eliminated entirely (‘pure-stack’). Emotions are almost completely absent from habits. The pure stack is invariable in length and depth and gets pegged to the time axis. This allows a pre-timing of events; the routine must only be initiated and can then be left unattended during execution. It can ‘bypass’ the here-now and frees up decision-making capacities. As a child, I was often told a story how employees in our local brewery used to drink abundantly while delivering barrels throughout town. After a hard day’s work the coachman would fall asleep on his box leaving it to the horses guiding carriage and driver back to the factory site. The decision-making process was outsourced, making space for dreams in the coachman’s minds. Of course, this tale breaks down at a key element — the coachman's brain is not the horse's brain — but it conveys very well the general idea — sometime we get home and we are unable to tell how we did so.

Young thoughts resemble the varying pattern of a river delta; mature thoughts can be compared to a canyon carved into the territory. Just as a river bed is carved out over centuries, there is no clear-cut delimitation between thought and habit. The process is fluent and habits form over time. When you reach home, you will most likely find yourself holding the right key already in your hand as you approach the front door; the complex pattern ‘reaching home’ has been paired with ‘getting keys’. You will not find yourself holding the keys in your hands when leaving through the front door (at least if you don’t lock your door like me): the ‘prepare-keys’ pattern is not simply pegged to the static pattern ‘your home façade’ but makes a dynamic distinction between reaching home and leaving. Similar event chains occur when you subconsciously log-in into your home or work computer; you will not accidently confound the passwords. The new-gained predictability comes at a cost: reliability is traded-off for flexibility. One possible following world-point state is much more likely than another. Automating the event chain removes it partly from the influence if the observer. If you will pull out your wrist watch and put it back into the pocket, you will notice that you haven’t really observed the actual time. To actively check the time, a pre-trained sequence must be interrupted by a conscious decision. Quirks related to automated sub-chains are numerous. If you write a word starting with the letter C within parenthesis, you will find that you will frequently skip the letter C; writing a similar shape unwillingly triggers the next letter. Speaking two similar languages can cause an unintentional swap between both when coming to a shared word. Habit sequences can unfold in short-term memory too. Sorting through a photo folder on your hard drive alternating between the delete key and arrow key, you will find that you hit one or the other involuntarily from time to time. Some more primitive habits include sneezing or laughing; once initiated the process, it’s hard to quit half way. The observer remembers points of decision (POD) along the mink-space path; in real life, we have better memory of intersections than of landmarks in between. This has consequence for the retrieval of habitual activities. We often remember that we picked up an object but don’t remember where we left it. In order to retrieve this information we must put us in the same situation again; per definition, trajectory and outcome are likely to be the same this time too. It also has an impact on time perception as only active here-now impressions count towards the perception of time.

New environments are the enemy of habits and favor malleability as the snatch-patch ratio increases. An unknown environment implies increased uncertainty, heightened alert, and more snatching. Thought patterns become less vertical and more horizontal. The number of outsourced patch reactions to external events inevitably decreases and the conscious mind must take over. New conditions require new assessments and external conditioning is lessened. A typical example would include tourist behavior abroad (getting drunk publicly, a tattoo etc. ).

‘Glitches’.

Even the most perfect system on the planet can fail occasionally; those instants of imperfection can offer valuable insight into its inner workings and allow testing our assumptions. As we have seen, fuzziness is built into the system; indeed, it's the cost of doing business. No two world states are identically the same, thus a system built on perfection and exact matches would fail utterly. Fuzziness lies at the heart of our creativity but is also the root of many mental shortcomings. False eyewitness testimony in police lineups is one example. If the suspect looks similar enough, the person could be identified as the perpetrator as the brain signals familiarity (not taking into account other factors like priming). The brain's laziness and preference for the 'naheliegende Lösung' (simple solution) can lead, especially under time pressure, to rash conclusions. Dean Buonomano illustrates this bug when he asks, “What does the cow drink?” The static patterns 'cow' and 'milk' are much more frequently compaginated than 'cow' and 'water'. Thus, milk is an easy hit in cow's stack bleed. 'Milk' is close enough, and therefore good enough, because it fits the static pattern 'liquid' and dynamic pattern 'drinking'. It takes more rolling operations (imagining the cow in its natural habitat) and effort to reach the correct answer 'water'.

During a process called snatch and patch, the mind picks up on umwelt signals and complements given information with the closest match. As the snatched image becomes more detailed, match accuracy increases. Sometimes, the amount of SWPs is limited by external factors and the matching process comes to an early halt. The ratio of PWPs for any given present plane is astonishingly high. From this perspective, déjà vus, hallucinations and flash backs can be seen as mental glitches that arise in the snatching-and-patching process. Flash backs are mink-space jumps induced by sufficient overlap with the present plane. It’s a true positive because the pattern has correctly identified; it’s just irrelevant to the current scene. Oftentimes, sounds or smells take us back to a situation witnessed earlier along the world line. In other situations, the event pattern shared by the present and past event plane might not be immediately obvious but nonetheless existent. Déjà vus are false negatives; a present-plane event topology is falsely identified and linked to a past event state which has never occurred. Hallucinations are the Trojan horse of pattern detection. A sufficient number of LLP is met to surpass a threshold and trigger a false-positive memory match. Hallucinations are not limited to visual perceptions; sometimes you might think to have heard or felt your mobile phone ring or vibrate because a subtle event pattern or pre-timed event tricked you into it.

Above we briefly discussed the case study of meeting a stranger in the street or mistaking a sports bag for a dog. Confusing a mannequin in a store window for a real person is another example. In those cases, real-world imagery was only temporarily limited. In other occasions, the quality of sensory input can be permanently impaired. One morning in low-light conditions, I reached out to grab the end of my USB charger which seemed to be lying at the edge of the matrass. It turned out that I fell for an optical illusion caused by shadows from a fold in the bed linen. We learned earlier that we are all experts at distinguishing spatial patterns; infinitesimal small changes in angle dф → 0 or distance dd → 0 are enough to discern relative positioning. Spatial illusions occur if a given static or dynamic pattern evokes the wrong response pattern. Reports of prisoners in solitary confinement portray how they create meaning where it cannot be found. Dancing dust clouds in the sun turn into vivid scenes before their eyes. Lack of sensory stimulation prompted the patching apparatus to go into overdrive. Travelers that get lost in a desert can suffer from wishful thinking too. In their case, not only the empty environment is to blame but in-org undernutrition can cut off or slow down signal processing too. Alcohol and drug consumption can also interfere with signal processing results in distorted mental imagery and hallucinations. Action movies frequently take advantage of the way the mind populates sensory gaps. In this case, in-between frames are embellished vertically. The spectator sees more than there actually is to see in the paused the motion picture. On a more abstract and more complex level, we can diagnose that conspiracy theories abound when the ruling class closes off access to information and the need for a meaningful environment is filled otherwise.

Sensory organs scan the umwelt selectively, the remaining present plane is hold in temporary memory just like a video-compression algorithm updates only changed sections of the frame. Magicians and pickpockets exploit the inertia in large portions of the mink space perception; they use misdirection to create perceptive holes in the desired region. The flashed-face illusion seems to be product of the same partial update process. While one region, or mid-level pattern (MLP), like eyes, mouth, or nose still adheres to the previous image, the other is already filled with the new picture leading to distortions in the TLP. Another situation — albeit on a larger time scale — in which we can observe a freeze frame is when we say goodbye to somebody at a street corner and expect the person still to be there when we return. We expect the world to pause in our absence and the last recorded picture is served back to our present plane when facing the same condition. The same thing happens when we wake up in a foreign place. The environment reassembles in the wake-up process: our body in a horizontal position, the mattress beneath us, everything is there. But we are totally bewildered because reality doesn’t match the patched image in our brain. Phantom limb syndrome could be attributed to persisting correlations between in in-org world points. The process consists of two stages and a double-patch reaction: In a first step, before the amputation, the physical world points in the limb get charged with connotation in the event stack through practice. In a latter phase, after the amputation, those connotations are evoked by other events, which shared the same compagination plane.

Event-related biases.

Detecting patterns is the first step in making sense of the environment. Salience has proven an effective tool distinguish features from their background; salience-related biases are plenty and often lead to an oversimplified world view. Observers focus on specific information and place too much importance of one aspect of an event, especially on those that stick out, are bizarre, more prominent or emotionally striking and ignore those that are unremarkable. A single policy might be credited for saving or ruining the entire economy; one player for success or failure of a whole team. Striving for pattern detection leads to tendency to seek even useless information, see patterns where there are none, such temporal and spatial runs, streaks, or clusters as well as none-existing relationships between them. Once a new pattern is learnt, the same pattern suddenly seems to appear with improbable frequency shortly afterwards, or the observer suffers from the illusion that the existence of the pattern is recent itself. Thus, expectation affects perception.

Umwelt-related biases.

The brain’s target function is to create the most coherent image of the umwelt possible. Environmental constraints and the complexity are causing noise in the result. A number of well-documented cognitive biases can be understood as repercussions of this over-eagerness to keep up with its job. Naïve realism is the belief that we see reality as it really is – objectively and without bias; that the facts are plain for all to see; that rational people will agree with us; and that those who don't are uninformed, lazy, irrational, or biased. The observer’s umwelt is its reality, a highly subjective matter. A dozen of other biases emphasize the ego-centric nature of the observer’s world view: memories relating to the self are better recalled than similar information relating to others; observers project and themselves as more singular than they actually are and perceive that their knowledge of their peers to surpass their peers' knowledge of them; they frequently overestimate the degree to which others agree with them and claim more responsibility for themselves for the results of a joint action than an outside observer would credit them with; they view themselves more variable in terms of personality, behavior, and mood while viewing others as much more predictable, further they over-emphasize any personality-based explanations for behaviors observed in others while under-emphasizing the role and power of situational influences on the same behavior.

Stack-related biases.

Other biases favor the present over the future and the status quo over the unknown in general. This can include undue liking for things merely because of familiarity. People tend to accept a statement as true if it is easier to process and are willing to believe something is true if their believe demands it to be true. They prefer to discuss information that all group members are already familiar with instead of information known only to a few. The evaluation of the logical strength of an argument is biased by the believability of the conclusion, and judgments are considered accurate when available information is consistent or inter-correlated. They focus on and remember information in a way that confirms their preconceptions and revise their beliefs insufficiently when presented with new evidence. They even reject such evidence when it contradicts a paradigm, and formerly hold positions emerge strengthened from a confrontation. Arguments however gain in plausibility through repetition in public discourse, and self-generated information is remembered better than imposed information. All these indicators point to a certain inertia associated with the event stack. New information needs docking points to form bonds with existing event patterns (adhesion). Successful ideas in terms of the observer’s weltbild, the world view cultivated from its umwelt, are such ideas that reconnect to existing ones. Thinking in general is exhaustive, but successful reconnections are rewarded with happiness (‘aha moments’). Ideas that match the prevalent weltbild are more likely to form bonds (latching) with the existing weltbild fabric then opposing concepts. Therefore, a change of mind requires steady persuasion over an extended period of time to willfully reshape the event stack.

Stack order plays an important role in decision making. The organism isn’t the same personality along its worldline and people oftentimes overestimate the likelihood of events with greater availability in memory. Regardless of those facts, people incorrectly remembering one's past attitudes and behavior as resembling present attitudes and behavior, thus they do not account for stack tilt. Furthermore, memory distortions introduced by the loss of details in a recollection over time, often concurrent with sharpening or selective recollection of certain details that take on exaggerated significance. We referred to this phenomenon as stack bend; some areas of the stack gross-section develop stronger than others. Other bias results from stack tinting: memory is becoming less accurate because of interference from post-event information confusing episodic memories with other information, creating distorted memories.

False memories find fertile ground where some reminiscent real memories are existent, but details have vanished. Old memories serve as anchor points (latches) for new information. Faded memories leave holes in the event fabric; the increased contact surface augments memory friction and facilitates interlacing of old and new imprints (adhesion). It is the brain’s task to vividly imagine the organism’s umwelt. Historically, it wasn’t so much concerned with producing veracious accounts of the past but to evaluate overall more likely scenarios. Those evolutionary priorities allow memory to be mistaken for imagination and imagination to be mistaken for a memory. It makes it possible that ideas suggested by a questioner are mistaken for memory. It also favors an “I-knew-it-all-along” attitude; it is more important that the real event chain sticks than a discarded chain of thought.

Bleed-related biases.

Many times, agents are reluctant to let go of a possession or self-made object because they have charged it with value in terms of connotations; if you like a special song it’s because it’s closely entangled with personal memories; if you defend your believes beyond objective reasons, it’s because they have become part of your umwelt fabric through a large number of bonds within your weltbild. If you are willing to purchase an overpriced piece of modern it’s less a matter of scarcity, personal price finding, or exceptional artistry, but rather has its value been charged through appraisal by role models whose role has been charged with credibility even before that.

A series of biases affect the matching and patching of snatched event patterns. Knowing an object will lead the observer to use it in the way it is traditionally used. Tool and application have been compaginated in the event stack and the observer is primed to use it in the usual way. ‘If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail’. In this case, nail has been positively primed by association with hammer, reducing the response time to bring it up as a solution; all other solutions have been negatively primed, increasing the time it takes to consider their usage. The sensory organs scan the environment and keep the mink-space image in working memory; snatched event patterns are matched and the corresponding event stacks are moved into working memory too. Event patterns that are activated and pre-fetched this way are predicted to be more likely to realize. The mechanism that is supposed to give the organism a competitive edge can turn into a self-fulfilling prophecy. Freudian slips are one manifestation of this predictive processing. I might sit opposite to a fat person, telling myself no to use the word ‘fat’, but it’s too late. The whole fat stack is already on top of my mind.

Everyday life is full of events that are shaped by subconscious instincts contained in the pre-fetched stack bleed. Impostors know how to surround themselves with shiny distractions like the famous Wilhelm Voigt who was depicted in the Captain of Köpenick; the 'uniform' pattern overshined the person beneath. One scene in a movie I was watching the other day illustrates the dynamics very well. An old man and a young man have the trunk of their car loaded with bags of drugs and are stopped by highway police. The young man approaches the police officer and tries to distract him unsuccessfully; the old man intervenes and leads the office to the back of the car, opens the lid before his eyes and invites the official to a pot of home-made jam. Obviously, the state trooper was put in high alert by the static event pattern ‘young man’ and the dynamic ‘distract-and-reject’ pattern. The ‘old-man pattern’ on the other conveyed helplessness and trustworthiness. The old man further deflates the tension and creates an amicable atmosphere by evoking the dynamic ‘invitation pattern’. He overrides the officer’s search instinct by doing his job for him. The drugs are right in front of the officer, but the trunk content was tinted with harmless ‘jam pattern’. Hence, the recipient of the action comes to different conclusions from the same information, depending on how that message is presented. Positive or negative spillover effects from one personality trait to another are frequently observed. It is also common to expect a member of a group to have certain characteristics without having actual information about that individual, or to consider characteristics of an individual group member to be reflective of that group as a whole.

The super-organism

Wave and particle.

The organism resides in its environment in constant exchange with its surroundings. It reacts upon external inputs and acts on them affecting the environment. Within the umwelt framework organism and environment are inevitably interlinked; the mind detects pattern, inspects them and reacts accordingly in constant exchange. Louis de Broglie brilliantly merged matter and wave into an inseparable unit. Immersing oneself into the congruous interplay among particle and wave in a pilot-wave setting, it is impossible to discern who sets the course: walker or field? The particle is deflected at the angle it hits the wave surface; and its impact will shape the wave it will land on the next time. No particle, no wave. The organism forms a similar symbiosis with its environment. The mind merely reacts on umwelt stimuli and shapes its surrounding at the same time. An organism creates and reshapes its own umwelt when it interacts with the world. This is termed a 'functional circle' by Jakob von Uexküll. The umwelt is for him an environment-world which is (according to Giorgio Agamben), “constituted by a more or less broad series of elements [called] ‘carriers of significance’ or ‘marks’ which are the only things that interest the animal”. The importance of those carriers of significance — event patterns — cannot be overstated.

Man-made patterns.

Andy Clark and David Chalmers proposed the concept of the Extended Mind where memory (e.g. phone numbers) or entire processes (e.g. navigation) are offloaded to external instruments: notepads, computers, and handheld devices. They therefore become part of the external mind of the observer. While they are right with their observations, the concept doesn’t go far enough. The relationship between mind and environment is much closer. Being implies a constant exchange of external and internal information, a steady cycle of snatch and patch; there simply can be no mind without the outside world. The extended-mind theory comes close to what I refer to as man-made patterns. Humans are the first animals that make extensive use of the power of umwelt marks. Animals convey information through posture, gestures, and facial expressions. They communicate through elementary vocal acoustic patterns and some even create chemical olfactory signatures that outlast the moment. These event patterns can persist remotely from the organism in mink space and be picked up from peers of the same species or predator and prey as well. Once an event pattern has been isolated and paired through compagination in the present plane, it enters the realm of consciousness (ROC) of the observer. Now the mark represents more than its physical boundaries in FOC; it opens a wormhole that reaches deep into mink space and evokes distant event clouds. The event stack is a rich well that provides the organism not with water but valuable information that can decide over life or death.

Hard and soft information.

We distinguish between hard and soft information. Soft information refers to the information contained in the event stack and is tied to the organism. Soft information is connotation and subject to constant change (mutable). Hard information refers to one snapshot of the event stack (immutable); it can be physically separated from the organism in mink space. Hard information is the way soft information is passed on from one organism to another. This transfer includes transfers to a future self. Durability is one big differentiator between hard and soft information: soft information dies with its bearer; hard information has no theoretical expiry date. Hard information undergoes a shift in connotation when it is re-paired; everybody hears something different when listening to the same explanations because no two organisms share the same worldline. Some connotations are universal (genetic) within a species, e.g. phobias; others are widely shared across a population (cultural), e.g. white as a symbol for purity, black for danger. For Joaquin Fuster, phyletic memory is the innate structure of the sensory and motor systems of the neonate. It is the memory of the species, acquired in the course of evolution from the interactions of the organism with its environment. Even for one and the same organism connotations changes: If you take a note today, it’s an excerpt of the current event stack; if you read it after one week you might not even remember what you had in mind. If you take a picture today, you hope to freeze all the positive emotions that went into it; if you look at the same picture thirty years later you might not even remember the people depictured on it. Even for the source organism, the limit of soft information for any given hard information equals zero as time approaches infinity.

Apart from those shortcomings, the impact of pattern creation has been huge for humanity. It allowed accrued learning for a single individual and across a population as whole and improved cooperation. Many thoughts don’t survive short-term memory; man-made patterns provided a means for repeated exposure and better chances to be stored into long-term memory. Note-taking and verbal exchange sped up learning as individuals no longer had to start over from zero at every recurring exposure. Pre-pairing of event patterns is how we remember, but at retrieval we recall general information first and might not even be able to recollect the details. The creation of ex-org patterns mitigated our mental weaknesses. For example, to remember a friend’s birthday we write it down in an agenda; if we want to recall this specific information, we know where to look for it because that’s where we usually store this kind of information.

Speech.

For the purpose of this analysis, we make an arbitrary distinction between speech and language. The advent of speech — defined as the ability to articulate and perceive specific sound patterns — has taken pattern creation to the next level. Every speech is closely tied to the natural environment; words can only be paired to what is present. Language is the first systemic approach of an organism to classify its umwelt. Most importantly, it adds the dimension of time to inter-org communication. Verbs created the ability to name dynamic event patterns. Mappings became increasingly granular leading to ever more fine-tuned sub-stacks. Even if every term requires a real-world blueprint, theoretical limits are endless: an infinite number of fractal-like sub-sets are yet to be discovered. Word evolution unfolds the same way biodiversity came to existence, by trial and error. A majority of words share the same fate as a lot species: only fossils remain. They were displaced and purged from the stock of living ‘breeds’. If any, hard information survived while connotation died. We can only speculate how the extinct word was pronounced and used, how the extinct species looked like and moved.

The process of word creation is primary snatching, or hatching — the first definition of an event pattern. Hatching occurs out of expediency and necessity. The stock of actively used patterns is not infinite; a certain frequency is required for a word to be practical and remembered. Its number grows and shrinks in accordance with the needs of the using population. It is important to mention, that the associated real-world pattern itself is not necessarily new when a pattern is hatched. It might always have been there; it simply has gained attention only as recently as now. At the same time, the existence of a word — that is, a pattern definition — patently shapes the umwelt perception of its observer. The sheer fact of bringing it into the consciousness of the observer, changes the reality of its observer (see section on biases above) — you don’t see it until you see it, and then you see it everywhere. Before a pattern has been delimited and named, it could have escaped the senses entirely or caused nothing more than a dull feeling (see emotions above). Hatching elevates an insignificant cloud of LLP world points into a higher order TLP formation. The brain dynamically tracks umwelt pattern in mink space. Speech expanded the set of traceable event patterns to higher-order immaterial topologies like luck, love, freedom, or happiness. Before a pattern is hatched it’s simply decoration; a defined and paired event pattern on the other hand opens up a universe behind it. An event pattern that has been delimited and paired now belongs to the observer’s realm of consciousness (ROC). New terms are frequently incorporated as loanwords into the vocabulary of a population; usually originating from the leitkultur — here defined as the leading culture in a certain field for a certain epoch — where most new discoveries are made. Adding a pattern to ROC can be a one-time event; removing it, usually is not. The organism cannot pretend not to know; learning a connotation is like eating the Forbidden Fruit from the Tree of Knowledge and being expulsed from the Garden Eden of naivety — there is no going back.

Just as any other event pattern, man-made patterns are bundled to another entity through repeated compagination in the present plane. Terrence Deacon calls this process grounding. Regular co-occurrence is the brain’s benchmark for a contextual or causal relationship between two events. Learning is intentional compagination; parents point to objects or depictions and pronounce their names. Once a word has been bundled to another event pattern, if one element is fetched from the event stack, the counterpart is evoked through rolling. When an adult learns a foreign language, a second layer of abstraction is added. The new word is frequently paired to the word in the native language, not the original impression. Grasping the content of a conversation or text in a foreign language includes filling the holes in the pattern fabric. Thus it is a combined process of jumping and rolling: known words in the surrounding of the unknown target term are translated into the source language (jumping), and frequently used neighboring terms are tested (rolling). When a candidate has been found, a new stack pair is created that will be reinforced or discarded in future occasions. Stack depth and stack breadth offer huge advantages to native speakers because wording is more fine-grained for a given situation. Foreigners on the other hand, frequently have a hard time to find the right word choice especially if stacks aren’t perfectly aligned, and one word doesn’t have a direct — or more than one — equivalent in the target language.

Language.

Language is our vehicle for spacetime travel. It allows us to visualize distant world-point topologies. Using a word evokes the compaginated real-world pattern and virtually recreates the mink-space landscape including smells and noises. Edmund Husserl calls this improper presenting; the account takes place outside of physical FOC. Grammar is modeled closely after mink-space architecture. Nouns are derived from static event patterns, and verbs from dynamic event patterns. The German word for word captures is essence: zeitwort — word of time. Toddlers tend to grasp static concepts faster than dynamic ones and we can speculate that nouns precede verbs in linguistic history due to a lower degree of complexity. Adjectives describe the qualitative sensory perception of an event. Grammatical tenses place events on the arrow of time and establish a chronological order. Subjunctive and conditional moods describe alternative world lines. Local prepositions are used to place an event in Euclidian space. Adverbs of frequency tell us how common an event pattern is. A complete phrase describes a compound pattern. The specific set of rules that makes a grammar work is rather arbitrary; many work. The final choice is nothing more than a societal convention that facilitates pattern detection of the written and spoken word. Respecting best practice of grammar or not is nothing more than difference between print and a bad handwriting; knowing where to expect a certain feature improves legibility. While language development is an unorderly (descriptive) process in general, grammar requires targeted (prescriptive) interventions to lift the system to a state of lower entropy much like shaping vegetation in a jardin à la française. Grammatical rules are laws; and laws — while following customs and traditions — must be codified from time to time.

Society.

The greatest value of man-made patterns is that they made information accruable and transferable. Now the organism could learn indirectly from the experiences of ancestors and contemporaries, and in turn pass own its own experiences to future generations. This effectively means removing any existing limits from the organism’s FOC. The observer’s sensory input is no longer confined to event patterns that lie within its field of perception. Information is passed on in a perpetual chain of soft information, hard information, soft information, and so on. To keep this chain of knowledge alive at least two members of any society must be alive at the same time. If the organism dies, soft information dies. Since soft information is mutable and depreciating, larger numbers significantly increase information stability and chances of survival. Societal shifts caused by external and internal factors like epidemics, migration, or occupation often bring one cultural period to an end. Soft information contained in its society goes extinct. In the long run all soft information dissipates and only hard information remains: We are left with ruins and relicts and can only wonder about the exact circumstances; we see the pyramids but cannot tell for sure how they were built; we find marginalia in medieval books and cannot make sense of them.

In this analysis we treat society itself as an organism: the super-organism (super-org). The super-set of all super-orgs is mankind. Obviously, the individual organism isn’t frequently exposed to humankind as whole; there are other sub-sets it shares a much closer relationship with: family, friends, classmates, colleagues, neighbors, and people who share the same nationality, language, ideology or religion. Many properties that apply to the organism, apply to the super-org in similar ways: It is a contingent event pattern in mink space; physical proximity and overlapping FOCs, is a pre-condition for soft information to be transferred from one organism to another (at least before the arrival of telecommunications; tactile and olfactory senses still require proximity). All super-orgs experience birth and death, even if the theoretical lifespan is infinite. Every super-org shows some degree of division of labor, that is acts in a eu-social manner. The prefrontal cortex is home to rational reasoning and long-term planning; it came last in evolution and the organism is able to survive without it, but it will fail immediately if it’s deprived of brainstem or cerebellum. Science can only build upon a functioning agricultural and productive society; society survives a loss of science but not a loss of food production. Super-orgs get hooked on feelings (e.g. war and victories), and suffer from collective biases (e.g. long-term planning is sacrificed for short-term pleasures).

Phi and Pi.

Giulio Tononi proposed Phi as a measure of consciousness for a given system; it denotes the amount of integrated information it possesses. Integrated Information Theory (IIT) proves compatible with the ancient doctrine of panpsychism that holds that consciousness is a property not just of brains but of all matter. Studied networks gain the highest phi possible if their parts are organized into separate clusters, which are then joined. If a system has Phi max = 0, meaning that its cause–effect power is completely reducible to that of its parts, it cannot lay claim to existing. If Phi max > 0, the system cannot be reduced to its parts, so it exists in and of itself. We could imagine a system where Pi denotes the observer’s FOC (pedio: Greek for field, domain, range). In accordance with our definition above, Pi = 0 for a time horizon of zero. Any system (organism) for which Pi > 0, is alive and conscious. The brain arranges information into stacks much like the clusters found in IIT. A deeper time horizon entails exponential growth of observable world patterns and the possible combinations of roll-and-jump relationships between them. Hard information is the means to make information durable and transferable; humans started to exploit this property, extending FOC well beyond established boundaries. The mink-space framework creates a quasi-panpsychic environment that tells a continuous but accelerating history on the evolution of mind while giving a reasonable explanation for the jump in consciousness at the same time.

Social learning.

Men became better at creating patterns; the mouth was able to produce ever more refined sounds, and hands were freed up from other tasks. Simultaneously, the ability to read and memorize patterns increased impressively. Humans were able to quickly absorb ever larger strings of information. Present-plane depth increased, and reliance on instant reflexes in the here-now and instincts declined. They kick in in time-critical situations when the survival of the organism is at risk. The human race went down the path that favored flexibility over perfect environmental pre-adaption. Post-adaption must be reasonably fast in order not to fall victim to the environment. Social learning was the tool that nature came up with to bring new-born humans up to speed in whatever environment they fell in. Other species do it too, but to a lesser degree. Social learning in general means unquestioned observation and emulation of senior group members. Ducklings, calves, cubs, and kids follow their parents’ tracks because they are considered save ground before choosing their own routes. Hence, learning is not purely behavioral; rather, it is a cognitive process that takes place in a social context. Social learning allows humans to draw from the repository of accrued knowledge. We call this reservoir culture and cultural evolution is a type of biological evolution.

Culture.

Humans are cultural animals. We are awfully unfit for survival when left to our own devices. The further removed our societal position is from forage and agriculture — let’s call it cultural entfremdung (German: alienation), in reference to Karl Marx — the more removed we are from the natural environment. Loss of instinct is the cost of the new-gained flexibility of the human race. We rely on the ‘collective brain’ in order to survive. Joseph Henrich uses this term elucidating the secret of our success: “It’s our collective brains operating over generations, and not the innate inventive power or creative abilities of individual brains that explain our species’ fancy technologies and massive ecological success.” He examines the cases of countless lost European explorers, highly trained and well-prepared expeditions that failed utterly, suffered death or were rescued from illness and starvation by indigenous people that possessed the necessary skills to survive in hostile territories. The intruders didn’t know how to fight extreme conditions, how to avoid poisonous plants or make them edible. The local tribes had acquired the necessary skills at a high toll of individual lives, but the super-organism as a whole stayed alive through trial and error and memorization of past mistakes.

Storytelling.

Before the invention of the printing press, storytelling was the most prolific way to spread information and keep the stock of accrued knowledge alive. Jamie Tehrani is one researcher that focuses on how culture evolves as it gets transmitted from person to person and from generation to generation; on the example of folktales he illustrates how culture evolves. Mark Turner explains how story and projection — and their powerful combination in parable — are fundamental to everyday thought. Storytelling allows passing large junks of coherent message from one organism to another. The internal logic of the story adds rigidity to the world-point cloud as a whole. One frame leads to the next; creating plenty of vertical overlap between images. Verbal embellishment and detailed portrayals add breadth to the cloud band. Increasing the surface of the event topology increases adhesion and improves chances that a certain piece of information sticks with the recipient; it makes it more likely that the audience can relate to some portion of the account. It is sufficient for one element to bond with the observer’s weltbild, to bind the remaining structure. From here on, the newly incorporated structure makes the listener more receptive for related content. Vertical integrity is one reason why recaps are so efficient; the observer fills in the gaps if presented with occasional milestones. Rhyming adds an additional layer to safeguard stiffness and integrity of the conveyed information that is mutable by nature. Acoustic pairs brace together consecutive lines, the first word primes the second one, making it easier for the narrator to fill in the gaps. We have seen that there is no objective reality and every enlargement to the existing fabric could be understood as progress by the observer. Arguably, the largest coherent patchwork of event patterns produced by mankind could be considered the most objective weltbild benchmark at every stage of history. The organism aims to complete its weltbild. Good information in the eyes of the observer is such information that is free of contradictions, that is, it reconnects seamlessly to mink-space tissue and doesn’t produce wrinkles and overlaps. Ironing out those wrinkles involves effort and frustration on behalf of the organism. Large coherent junks have an intrinsic inertia and are harder to be overturned. The brain needs steady persuasion in order not to get frustrated with an overload of failed connections. One could view religion as the most successful story ever told; a more or less coherent fabrication of laws, rules, best practices, experiences, and scientific discoveries delivered in an entertaining format. Up to the present day, storytelling plays an important role shaping the public perception: Hollywood, Netflix, and Instagram set the narrative, values and the political agenda.

Over-imitation.

When ducklings, calves, cubs, and kids follow their parents’ footsteps, they do not question their decision. Humans are not so much different in that they copy behavior first and ask questions later (or never). Children learn by imitating adults — so much so that they will rethink how an object works if they observe an adult taking unnecessary steps when using that object. Take for example the case where you teach a toddler a new task; if the parent claps after successful completion, the toddler will include clapping into the routine. “Even when you add time pressure, or warn the children not to do the unnecessary actions, they seem unable to avoid reproducing the adult’s irrelevant actions,” says Derek Lyons. Over-imitation is not reduced to childhood but extends over the whole life time of the organism. It is a shortcut to catch up on the accumulated knowledge in society; the organism copies and pastes whole sub-routines into its own mental and behavioral processes. Those modules don’t need to be perfect in the first place; they are optimized if necessary at a later stage. Over-imitation includes copying wasteful or inefficient practices especially if those actions are carried out by a hero. A hero is any kind of higher ranking role model. First and foremost, the brain is a dynamic tool; vertically chaining sequences is what it does. The organism tracks ex-org world points like prey and predator; it produces in-org movement. In both cases we deal with continuous wp-cloud bands, no unexpected jumps involved. Not only do humans study behavior through observation, they internalize consequences of the observed behavior too (vicarious reinforcement).

Over-imitation is deeply engrained and reaches low-level habits like yawning and sneezing. The observer does it because a peer does it, without no felt need whatsoever just an instant ago. Over-imitation has a series of important consequences: It shatters another aspect of homo economicus; do first, think later is incompatible with rational behavior. It reduces the perception of free will even further; maybe we don’t want to become like our parents, but we have no other choice because we don’t know better. It can be seen as a driving force for gleichschaltung (German: conformation) within a society and major cause for over- and undershooting on markets; the hero leads (for good or bad reasons), the herd follows. Gleichschaltung can be associated with a number of negative outcomes, aggravated by the multiplying factors of mass media and social media: stardom and winner-takes-all markets. But its uniting evolutionary upside cannot be neglected. It serves as a societal binder and turns centrifugal forces into an aligned stream and group members into a united team. To put it simple, it’s better to have a stupid idea the group can unite behind together than no sense of togetherness at all.

Fate.

All human societies have developed the concept of fate. When we discussed malleability we saw how little was left of our initial notion of free will. We detect and react to umwelt signals. Our past world line determines how exactly we react to a given pattern in the present plane. In the preceding paragraph, over-imitation squashed what was left of self-determination even further. We just copy entire junks of other organisms’ behavior, no questions asked. After a while we even think the initial idea was ours. The ideal of our originality appears to be a myth; birds in a cage like Skinner’s pigeons would be a more appropriate picture of us. Our neighbor buys a SUV, so do we; gets a dog, so do we; gets a tattoo, so do we. Our teacher believes in Newton’s laws, so do we. Give a man a castle and he will start acting like a king. The Stanford prison experiment has shown that agents fall into assigned roles. The experiment was interrupted prematurely, but over-imitation suggests that more of the good guards would have turned. We behave as (implicitly) told. Are we just shell actors that fill a space, led by larger circumstances? Can we observe fate and predetermination on a global scale? Nothing is as powerful as an idea whose time has come, they say. The right idea at the wrong time probably will not grow on but go unnoticed due to the lack of friction in mink space. On the other hands, what’s due to materialize because the time is ripe, will materialize. Take for example the groundbreaking theses of Charles Darwin, if he hadn’t finished his work, Alfred Russel Wallace would have filled the void. There seems to be a general principle. The world sets its own pace; you can speed but others will catch up at the next red light, you can amble and still join the group at dawn.

Thought

Vertical and horizontal thought.

If we try to trace down our thoughts in mink space and map the location of event patterns that get called a series of simplified thought structures emerge. Trajectories close to the here-now usually affect the organism itself, being located in spatial proximity and of infinitely small time horizon their only concern is the very next event state. It’s the mechanism that perpetuates the organism forward on its world line like a millwheel. The processes are highly vertical and time sensitive; the instructions are simple and down-stack. From this, that. Both the respiratory system and circulatory system are very down-stack. The motor system already involves some horizontal elements: ex-org patterns are snatched and tracked. But the vertical component still predominates: in-org patterns must be instructed, timing is critical for motion. From here we come to spatial understanding; the horizontal thought component gains importance. The broader picture starts to matter; the vertical component no longer is the dominant factor: Figuring out how to get from London to Paris, time is only one concern (compare route planning to overtaking on a highway). The further we move into abstract and logical thinking, the less time-critical it becomes. The horizontal component gains compared to the vertical aspect. Think of a math problem, time doesn’t matter to figure out one plus one. In reality, all thinking is triangular; it originates in the here-now, undergoes some operation, and is returned to the here-now as output. This is similar to John Stuart Mill's principle of obliviscence in his critical examination of William Hamilton’s philosophy. According to this principle, not all the elements that generate an idea need to appear in consciousness; what does appear is only the end product of what might be a long chain of associated elements. On an endnote, we could rephrase horizontal thinking mnemonically as an activity extending from the cerebrum into the wide and vertical thinking as instructions that are sent down from the cerebellum to the spine.

Thought patterns.

Events are tracked and extrapolated into the future. Concentrated reasoning maintains a strong link to the here-now. The thought pattern takes the shape of an hourglass; events are tracked, processed, and returned for output to the here-now. Creativity is less time critical and much more horizontal. Its Minkowski pattern now takes the shape of a radial polygon. Cross connections are made, some of them continue to run through the here-now. Douglas Hofstadter names analogy as the core of cognition. These horizontal links are the means by which we produce such analogies: They put us in the position to recognize patterns between seemingly unrelated events and to project the worldline of an observed on a new situation. Dropping alertness causes thoughts to stray and wander off the world line. Sensory input draws attention to the mink-space proximity and turn thoughts inbound. A free climber is highly focused on the immediate surroundings; only the next step matters. He enters a flow-state of mind; thinking is locked down to the body and the umwelt under his hands and feet. He hovers over the rock like a donkey over a narrow and rocky trail in the Andes. The flow state of mind is the closest the human mind can get to the animal psyche; fears and worries usually tagged to the situation disappear. Sleep on the other end, large shuts down sensory inputs and bodily controls. The mind is widely disconnected from the world line; the result is a reality exempt from physical constraints. Other interesting states in the middle ground can be looked into. Children enjoy carefree play not least because their FOC is still in development and time horizon limited. Sports also heavily rely on in-orgs and comprise a limited range of ex-orgs.

Concentrated thinking is characterized by a steady back and forth between the here-now and remote world points; thought conclusions are constantly checked against reality. Another very interesting case concerns daydreaming: a sufficiently demanding task keeps the organism awake. The task is highly automated (habit) that can be left unattended with little here-now interventions. Such activities include walking or cycling. Solvitur ambulando is a Latin phrase which means ‘solved by walking’. A ‘naheliegende Lösung’ in German means an obvious solution but literally translates to the ‘nearby solution’. Solvitur ambulando we don’t restraint ourselves to the obvious but come up with surprisingly creative proposals. Most of our best ideas come while doing simple but unrelated activities like walking or riding the bicycle. This is no coincidence. While conscious activities like reading demand constant connectivity to the here-now, those tasks are sufficiently automated and can be left unsupervised. At the same time, the activity is sufficiently demanding compared to sitting or lying not to fall asleep. Thus it allows highly productive cognitive thinking to kick in and be sustained for prolonged periods of time. The thought pattern can be visualized by a highly interwoven spatial mesh that is greatly detached from the here-now. Dream activity at night is most time insensitive; large waves waft in remote orbits from the here-now touching distant world points. We are mostly paralyzed while a small pilot light in the spacetime origin orchestrates basic biological functions. If this lights goes out we die, while long-lasting, low-frequency waves will likely continue to reverberate beyond the instance of death. Thus, the field of consciousness will collapse from the inside out the moment an organism dies. This visualization of thought patterns in timespace offers an elegant way to address the integration problem of dreaming raised by Jennifer Windt and Valdas Noreika.

A coincidence?

At this point it is worth to point out an uncorroborated but eye catching correlation between thought patterns and measured brain activity. Brain activity is commonly categorized into gamma, beta, alpha, theta, and delta waves. From physics we know that short wave lengths have higher frequencies and penetrate lesser distances as their energy gets dissipated faster. Longer waves travel longer distances at the expense of lower frequencies. At lower natural frequency, less energy is released per unit of time to the surrounding medium. In telecommunications, we use wave to transport information. Higher frequencies can accommodate higher data rates at the expense of inferior reach and longevity. Curiously enough, the time-distance pattern perfectly matches our findings regarding the thought patterns in mink space. Further-away memories go along with low-frequency waves. Furthermore, world points close to the here-now are time-critical and possess high data density. Much of this information needed is short-lived and discarded rapidly. Right now, I’m perfectly aware of any key I hit on my keyboard and any alternative thought or sentence structure that crossed my mind. Tomorrow, I will only remember the table I was sitting at, and after one year only the final document will be left.

Thought chains.

Thoughts can take two routes through mink space: rolling or jumping:‘from this, that’ and, ‘this’ like that’. Rolling links compaginated events; that is, adjacent event patterns in mink space. The thought band chooses the route of strongest overlap. Adjacency can be horizontal (conjunction) — where there are palms there is water — and vertical (consequence) — thunder follows lightning. The other brain route is jumping (analogy); that is, the brain links alike-shaped event patterns — the Louvre reminds us of Egyptian pyramids. All thought follows these instructions. Analogy makes metaphors and parables such powerful linguistic tools; they map complex event patterns to a commonly known and understandable dynamic picture. If we imagine a three-dimensional body in space it’s a rolling instance. We previously walked around the object and now repeat this exercise mentally chaining horizontal event patterns. Mental arithmetic follows the same laws: From 1 follows 2. And, 1 to 10 like 2 to 20. We call the route a thought takes through mink space a thought chain because every two elements share a common overlap like elements in a chain.

Latching.

Thinking is like laying domino bricks. Laying bricks is exhausting, so is thinking and organisms prefer to avoid it. We prefer the well-known (faces, languages, ideologies etc.) and are pleased by symmetry. Symmetry cuts down n rolling operations to n/2 rolling operations and one jumping operation. If a thought chain reconnects, we call this process latching — usually referred to as an ‘aha moment’. Successful latching is rewarded by the brain with a rush of happiness making the event more memorable (see emotions). Active learning as proposed by Carl Wieman uses this fact; the teacher doesn’t present answers but is guiding the hunt by answering the students’ questions. Latching is fitting in the missing puzzle piece. The versed puzzle player sorts pieces by shading and shape in a first step, much like the brain sorts even patterns into event stacks. From there she makes her moves. When we try to remember, we push domino cues into all direction starting from a nearby ground hoping to reconnect to known pattern. Most trails come to nothing, but more initial cues increase the chances. This makes recalling so much more difficult and frustrating than recognition; we must advance the mine not knowing where we will hit the lode. Once we hit the pot, latching reaffirms the find. Recall of an item from memory requires a two-stage process in which the search and retrieval of candidate items from memory is followed by a familiarity decision where the correct information is chosen from the candidates retrieved.

Latching is the second means to augment ROC; hatching being the first. Hatching is the delimitation and pairing of a pre-existing event pattern (discovery). Latching is the re-shaping or re-arrangement of one or more pre-existing event patterns (invention). It is the same driving force behind in-org assembly, better known as biological evolution. The curse of entropy dictates that there are only so much world states that work but an infinite number of combinations that don’t. We are intrinsically bad at predicting outcomes because odds are stacked against us. The outcome drives the process, and not vice versa. Invention can only ever be an incremental process. Earlier we saw how the mind came up with over-imitation to avoid constant failure. Primary latching refers to the super-organism, a world first; we speak of secondary latching if the individual organism comes to same conclusion on its own.

We possess a spatial perception of event patterns that allows us to mentally scale, stretch or rotate them. We use this capability to reassemble and shape our environment, to fit clothes in suitcase and a pile of bags in the trunk of our car. Pattern transformations are the result rolling operations on prior mental imagery we possess of alike-shaped objects (jumping). This footage results from physically changing distance and viewing angle in reference to the object, comparable to panning, tilting and dollying of a camera. The sculptor, to come back to our previous example, is able to comprehend his artwork three-dimensionally because he has walked around it, stepped back and inspected it up-close. Indeed, we are much better at mentally scaling of images than at rotating them, simply because we are not accustomed to observe our world upside down. Strictly speaking, nothing has ever been invented out of thin air; all invention is evolution of the existing (incremental change, genealogy). Let’s re-imagine the invention of the wheel: On day one, Newton is sitting below an apple tree observing a round fruit rolling down-hill. On day two, Newton is ordered to carry object from A to B. he finds a large uncut log beside the road. The rest is history. History of art is full of match-and-latch. Architectural monuments endure time and line out a path of slightly different variations that end up being something unrecognizably centuries later. A New-York-Times column called Diary of Song, portraits the making of a piece of music. Copy-pasting existing elements, stretching, and filtering are the ingredients of a new song or genre. People avoid mental effort but enjoy the recompense of discovering something new (latching). A successful new song should offer a small surprise but must not be out of step with its time to please its listeners.

The field of thought (FOT).

A person’s realm of thought (ROC) determines its field of thought (FOT). The FOT is the ambit where the organism’s thought building process takes place. Concatenation prowess (CP) is the organism’s ability to form and sustain chains of thought. The overall level of CP roughly corresponds to our notion of intelligence. The FOT landscape varies wildly among humans, and its topology shapes the type of intelligence. If CP is the amount of water that flows down a river, than FOT is the shape of its river bed. The river bed can take the shape of a canyon — guiding its current over the centuries — or of a river delta — taking a different layout every season. Usually delta-shaped layouts extend much further into mink space than valley-shaped courses. An ample time horizon (that reaches from the big bang to the ultimate fate of the universe) is one of the most distinguishing features between humans and animals. Time mitigation and pondering of alternatives became an inevitable necessity especially in new, unknown situations. The prefrontal cortex is its material manifestation. This moment of thought and doubt was inserted upstream in the decision-making process before final actions are taken. The prefrontal cortex not only sits physically in front of older brain regions; its responsibilities precede other thought processes in time-space continuum. It is only over-ruled under conditions of ultimate stress and deprivation. Tooby, Cosmides, Pinker et al call the human-specific faculties improvisational intelligence as opposed to dedicated intelligence.

Forms of intelligence.

If an organism is good at something; it’s effective at connecting ponds of information (concatenation prowess). A priori there is no superior FOT layout. Both tight-knit, compact and far-reaching, complex layouts offer benefits and drawbacks. Different thought patterns proof advantageous under different external conditions. And external conditions are prone to change. A species might be perfectly adapted up until an invasive species turns its habitat upside-down. Under regular circumstances, society draws strength from a broad spread of possible FOT realizations. Eu-social behavior results in division of labor giving strength to the colony. Simply put, a simple network belongs to a simple mind while a complex network belongs to a complex mind. Bayesian probabilities level the playing field among the entire spectrum. Close world points have a high probability to materialize while distant world points are highly hypothetical. Complex minds often get stuck in the thought. Entropy laws make it increasingly difficult that all switches are on green; higher levels of doubt in the equation lead to higher diffidence. Horizontal thinking acts as a break. Simple minds are executive, vertically aligned workers that are occupied with implementing the task at hand. Vertical thinking minimizes drag and streamlines execution.

FOT and personality.

We can cautiously utter some generalizations regarding both prototypes. Personalities with a small FOT footprint tend to be extrovert but less sensitive to their environment at the same time. A smaller contact surface reduces friction and makes it harder to detect umwelt nuances. Organisms with a large FOT footprint are generally more open-minded while small FOTs face higher resistance to break out of their fairway. By the same token, small FOTs tend to be more risk averse than big FOTs. Small FOTs are rule-based personalities while big FOTs are more experimental and creative. Game-theoretic pay-offs for both strategies are widely different; time horizon dictates that short-FOT strategies face almost immediate reward or punishment. Verdicts whether a long-FOT opinion was right or wrong can be delivered well beyond the organism’s lifetime. Intelligence can be described as concatenation prowess; it comes in many forms: motor intelligence, social intelligence, or intellectuality. The classical notion of intelligence could be best translated into foresightedness in mink space. Popular board and card games owe their success to how they utilize the brain’s event-tracking capacity and predictive nature to compete in a controlled environment. Organisms self-select into assignments that best fit their FOT. For a football player, shortsightedness can prove a competitive advantage over the opponent. Not to worry about family problems at home, the opinion of tomorrow’s press, or yesterday’s criticism, will allow the player to focus on the task at hand. Similarly, for a novel writer or scientist it can prove to be advantageous to let the thoughts run free and ‘to be spaced out’ and not to focus on the here-now.

Dreams.

At day time, senses constantly scan the environment painting the organism’s umwelt (snatching). Gaps in the mental image are filled and information is complemented with past experiences through rolling and jumping (patching). The space-temporal focal length shifts widely for different thought patterns and the organism’s umwelt perception is a co-product of snatching and patching. Highly demanding physical activity strongly cuts back FOT; all thinking is inbound to world line. In this case, the share of snatched elements at overall consciousness is highest, matched elements are limited to a minimum. Actions are highly space-time critical. For daydreaming on the other end the share of matched elements is high, snatched elements make up only a limited portion of the observer’s reality; only few world points from the present plane make it to the foreground of consciousness; most elements stem from distant mink-space locations. Dreams are almost completely cut off from the world line and outbound. Their stories have mystified humans throughout history. The mink-space framework accommodates dreaming seamlessly into a broader range of mental activity. Present-plane input drops even further and the vastness of mink space takes over. Most likely was dreaming an accidental evolutionary by-product of corporal downtime imposed by dark hours (or light hours, for nocturnal creatures), but soon after it earned its very own raison d'être.

Link to the present.

If we wake up from deep sleep (NREM) we often feel muzzy and disoriented. We ‘need a few seconds’ to get on our feet. Under normal circumstances, light sleep (REM) prepares us to reconnect to the umwelt; our patching engine starts up. Dreaming is patching unrestrained from the physical environment. Sometimes the environment breaks through and we incorporate real-life events in our dreams. In this case, patched dreaming reality assembles seamlessly around the intruding event pattern. Those incorporated events are a first timid attempt to reattach to the world. Incorporated event patterns can originate from in-org or ex-org world points. A few weeks ago I dreamt that a dog was holding my arms in his mouth when indeed I was lying on it cutting off blow circulation. A few days later, a couple was arguing loudly in the street below my window; again, the voice found their way into my dreams. One of the best examples includes a plane ride across the Atlantic. We were crossing some turbulence with sudden drops in altitude. I knew this rollercoaster feeling all too well: as a small child my mother would drop me and my brothers off at grandmother’s place. On the way there, the road crossed a parabolic embankment. My brothers and I loved the feeling of zero gravity and encouraged my mother to speed up just before crossing the ridge. Now back in the plane, I was dreaming to fly with my brothers along the same street just a few feet above the ground, not in a car but in an aircraft! Sometimes the call from the outside world is not so obvious but the umwelt wants to get the mind’s attention nonetheless. After a hearty, salty dinner and wine at the Greek restaurant around the corner, my body craves for water and needs my intervention earlier than usual. Nightmares are its way to get my attention and wake me up just after midnight. The internal clock is another means for the body to get in touch with the mind: The other morning I was sleeping longer than usual and the time I usually take my shower I was actually dreaming to have a shower.

Dream structures.

From my personal experience, all dreams are structured in a similar ways and the similarity with cinematographic techniques is striking. The question is: who copied who? Or has the length of a scene and the number of featured events taking place at the same time something to do with our natural attention span (present-plane depth) and the intrinsic amount of traceable threads? Dreaming is umwelt specific. The organism can only dream in accordance with its sensory feeds in a wakeful state. Those sensory inputs nourish the iceberg that is pushed below the surface as the past event stack grows. For humans, seeing, hearing and emotions compose the majority of dreams. Only few of us experience smell in their dreams; those are usually people with highly developed olfactory senses working in scent related fields. Immanuel Kant, the German philosopher, called smell the “least rewarding and most easily dispensable” of our senses. Every scene contains a number of protagonistic event patterns which can be static, dynamic or complex event patterns. Those TLP are generally spot-on and it’s perfectly clear who or what was depictured; behavior, character, and even word choice for a person are infallible. The same cannot be said for LLP that compose the visual appearance of the TLP or the backdrop of the scenery. Usually it’s unclear where a setting originates from. Most likely it has been composed from scratch up. LLP can be freely rearranged but generally their position makes sense. The other day, very long eyebrows on a man beside me caught my attention during a two-hour bus ride; a few weeks later I saw the same eyebrows on the face of a friend of the family in one of my dreams. Defining properties for a TLP are maintained but LLP can be swapped on the go: a dog can take the shape of a wolf from one frame to the next, a car turn into a go-cart — objects from different ramifications of the same stack stem that share a broad range of commonalities.

Dream content.

Most dreams are not remembered at all; for an imagery to be conscious, it must be delivered to the here-now. Two factors help holding a dream in the foreground for a little longer. The first means is a distraction-free environment. Dark and quiet surroundings reduce the peril of salient event patterns that hijack our senses, lowering the amount of snatching overrides, and keeping the freeze frames of the dream intact. On the same note, Robert Nash found eye-witness accounts to be more accurate if the respondents keep their eyes shut due to fewer distractions. The second vehicle is emotions. We saw earlier that emotions are a chemical cocktail that outlasts the moment. Concurrent events get pegged to in-org emotions event patterns and are held in focus. Emotions are also a driver of content choice for upcoming dreams. In my case, every dream features recent event patterns from the previous wake cycle. Those patterns that the brain deems noteworthy don’t follow our standards of importance but are likely to adhere to criteria of newness, surprise and other emotional responses. Sometimes the transferred event structure is obvious; in other cases the stack connection is fuzzy. I found it possible to train pattern detection by learning how the surrounding event fabric ‘feels’. Recreating the conditions will unearth the missing piece.

Purpose and meaning of dreams.

Philosophic and scientific explanations on the role of dreams have been abundant over the course of human history. Some of the prevalent contemporary theories fit well into the mink-space framework, memory formation and consolidation, or problem solving to name just a few of them. In our dreams, we regularly blend current event patterns with event-space snippets from the past event cone. When puzzle players open a new game they will likely start sorting the pieces by color tone or shape outline. In a dream an event pattern is stripped of its observed surrounding event tissue and fitted into a new context. It is grouped with its alike-shaped event patterns in the same pile. We refer to this process as reverse matching, or stacking. From the stack, it latches out and bonds with bleed patterns from a different time plane. This diagonal compagination of event patterns results in cross-temporal stack integration; events get rooted in the stack. Observed patterns reflect the existing world. In our dreams, the compagination process is unleashed, and unheard-of combinations can be created. Dreams are not limited to be timelike, but some of the dreamt-up topologies, or their alike-shaped metaphors, will fall into the future light cone and prove applicable in real life. Thereby, those novel cross-temporal and cross-spatial links make latching happen and contribute to diversification and genealogic, incremental ROC growth. During the day, event flow reverses bottom up — past to future — and allows making projections into the future. Diagonal thinking can be seen as one of the most powerful tools for problem solving (latching). Dreaming excites event patterns that connect well to sensory information from the day before; in the process, dreams refresh relevant memory and make it readily available (priming). This activity can be very subtle and low-level; the first sunrays of the year or the smell of freshly mown grass and young flowers can serve up information from a comparable situation last year. The traditional Freudian view of dreams as the Royal Road to the unconscious is likely true to the extent that those stack patterns lie dormant until they are awakened. If Freud suspects that they reveal our deepest desires, it may only apply to the same degree as wishful-thinking biases in a wakeful state. Wishful thinking and preference for known patterns stand at the core of umwelt recognition. It is easier to find something if you know what you are looking for: It is easier to spot the milk in the fridge if you know what the container looks like, and you will probably scan the ground in hope to spot a crumpled Dollar bill and not a piece of thrown-away gum wrapper.

The global sleep cycle.

Sleep emerged in co-evolution with the brain and plays a crucial role for thought. Sleep is qualified into different stages whose exact purpose is not fully understood. Looking at the global sleep cycle (the organism’s lifespan) and the local sleep cycle (the organism’s circadian rhythm), some basic necessities at every stage, and basic laws of life we can express some brief and cautious guesses. During the global sleep cycle we can observe a thinning out of the sleep pattern — sleep loses its edges and both very deep N3 and light REM sleep decrease over time. While adults have to fulfill their role as protector and provider of the family, the offspring has to absorb a huge cognitive load. And young humans must learn a lot in their early life, from facial recognition, controlling motion, to their mother language. Both passive pattern recognition and active pattern creation must be learned. We assumed dreaming to be the primary vehicle for memory stack formation and the global thought pattern confirms this suspicion. Starting from near zero, trial and error is high. Pattern optimization includes separating the wheat from the chaff the same way a sculptor isolates the statuary from the surrounding rock. Habit formation means finding the optimal path through mink space; new trails are tried out and discarded before the preferred choice is found. This process of trying and erring should be reflected in heightened synaptic restructuring; many void attempts must be pruned. We could conclude that the hard work of restructuring is done during deep sleep.

The local sleep cycle.

The local sleep cycle shows a preponderance of deeper sleep at the beginning and lighter sleep towards the end. It makes sense to split the overall circle into a series of self-containing sub-circles. Environmental insecurity increases during the course of the night, and the probability to reach certain sleep duration decreases. Splitting up the overall volume into smaller units improves chances to cover at least some desired value of all types of sleeps and obtain the potential biological benefits. Another upside of sub-cycles is due to the fact that humans are social animals. Larger groups increase chances that sleep cycles are not perfectly aligned. Phase offset guarantees that at least some group members are more alert to external dangers. Early sleep cycles put emphasis on deeper sleep; later cycles invert the ratio. One of nature’s basic principles can be translated into the phrase ‘there’s no tomorrow without today’. The organism always shifts preferences towards survival in the presence. For sleep, this means restoring readiness before improving performance — ‘drying the sponge before growing the sponge’. Therefore we can conclude that N1 and N2 are mainly concerned with flushing short-term impressions that seem unworthy to be passed onto long-term memory and resetting the emotional plate of instructions. We have seen that emotions are responsible for tracking event threads and priority management. Accumulation of the chemical footprint results in higher levels of stress and temper. Sleep is a stress reliever. After cleansing, only the most unusual and remarkable events are left when the mind enters REM sleep. For adults of the same age group, we could assume that creative and pensative people who maintain a large FOT need more sleep than executive routinists. Even the same adult might sleep longer hours during vacation; maybe due to less worries, more fresh air and physical exercise, but maybe also because more new sensory impressions must be screened and archived.

The brain

From mink space to the brain.

The observer’s position is the origin of mink space. The organism is the center of its umwelt and we can read all coordinates as relative to the here-now. Real-world events correspond to event topologies that emerge in the observer’s FOC. We have grouped repeating, isolated event patterns into event stacks. The flattened event stack returns the event mold, or event envelope. Constructing this mental entity, we’ve got one step closer to mapping the real world to the brain. We’ve successfully condensed an infinite space into a line, and finally into a globe-like object. The refinement process for creating unique event molds is astoundingly accurate but not infallible. Objects that are not the same but share common features such as color, size, relative position to other events, or motion patterns do share overlapping areas in the envelope. This fuzziness leads to fallacies in recognition like me to mistake a bag for a dog. Bleed, or context, is indispensable in the process. A pony shape is a pony if it has its four feet on the ground, no matter if it’s standing in front of us or depicted in a book. If it’s rising or fluttering in the air, it’s pony-shaped balloon. The fact that we consider event motion results from present-plane depth and means that we must include the vertical (time axis) in the event mold. We can imagine it as one of those iPhone photos where you can hover over and it shows you the short before and after. Thought chains are series of overlapping event patterns and the corresponding bleed through rolling and jumping. They frequently originate from sensory inputs, and then propagate through mink space until they finally abate, or reconnect (latch) and reverse. In the world of the event mold, space is no longer limitless in any direction; thought chains are no longer cloud bands of world points but alternating constellations in place.

Brain constellations.

Analyzing thought patterns above, we discovered an odd correlation of distant world points being associated with long, low-frequency wave lengths and world points close to the observer with short, high frequency wave lengths. At any point along the world line the observer’s FOT is characterized by a unique footprint. This complex wave pattern could be broken down into its building blocks (the 'Brain (Wave) Alphabet’) from which any imaginable sentence or world state could be recomposed. If we could recreate the original footprint, we would re-create the same umwelt state. We would indeed have created memory of that event state. Entropy laws make it impossible to recreate the exact same conditions but we can aim for partial reproductions. Memories fade (decay) and get mixed up with other events (interference). As a thought (or dream) chain crosses an event pattern, world points in this pattern are associated with new event patterns (compaginated) altering their context. We could imagine that world points are refreshed in the process gaining a bump in amplitude which helps to delay their final decay. Not all umwelt impressions are treated equally; some stick right away others don’t. We could speculate if this behavior is owed to the unsteady nature of waves; the bump relayed to the next neuron in the instant of connection could depend on the current position in the wave period. In this case, randomness would assume the role as a gatekeeper for what is deemed relevant in the long run. Building consciousness on waves requires a constant energy input to keep memories alive. We are assigning a variable cost to the maintenance of information. If the energy supply dries up, the field of consciousness collapses, time horizon is reduced to zero, and by definition, the organism is no longer alive. From the nature of wave lengths we suspect that the field of consciousness will implode from the inside out in the event of death. Short-lived, high-energy waves in the cerebellum will cease to exist while slow waves in the prefrontal cortex will reverberate for some instances longer. A dead world point will probably be stripped off its compagination magic for good, dooming ideas to conserve conscious brains beyond death. Summary

Closing words.

We learned that the brain is mainly a dynamic instrument and even called it the organ of time. Time perception grew in lock step with sensory penetration of the environment. Language broke down the last barrier and converted the brain into a human vessel for time travel and made time horizon almost infinite. In the course of this essay we have seen that the brain is charged with two tasks: make sense of the environment and produce sensible responses to environmental conditions. It does so relying on the organism’s sensory inputs. To fulfill its duties, the brain relies on two simple operations: co-occurrence and analogy which result in two possible thought-chain concatenations, rolling and jumping. The co-occurrence rule (means 1) states that world points that appear or move together in mink space most likely belong together. World points compaginated in such a way, are further refined and form a (static, dynamic, or complex) event pattern. Similar event patterns are grouped into event stacks. The event stack ramifies in a tree-like manner and allows the brain to jump to the next similar event pattern and draw analogies to past experiences (means 2). Rolling is not limited to horizontal (concurrent) world points in the present plane but can take in a vertical component along the time axis. Both means combined allow the brain to judge unknown situations, make assumptions about causality, and predictions about the future.

Our awareness arises in the morning as the field of consciousness assembles and our state becomes increasingly clear. “Where am I? How did I get here?” Our personality takes shape intertemporal differential analysis of world states. Much like our physical environment takes shape when touched by the first rays of sun and the chants of birds from the treetops. At night the scenery lies dormant veiled in darkness and silence. Only once light touches its surface, space is sculptured by gradients, edges, and tonal contrasts. The surrounding becomes increasingly detailed; equally our awareness increases as the field of consciousness is generated. The mind of a single organism lights up small ambit in its close proximity, the umwelt; the organism’s world line could be seen as a firefly’s journey through the dark of the night. Maybe there is a better road out there, but the organism can only be aware of what's illuminated by the headlights of its vehicle — its field of consciousness. It acts and reacts upon events within the scope of its awareness. Language made knowledge transferable and cumulative. Society could start sharing information and passing it on to subsequent generations. The trace of a single firefly turned into a cloud of fireworks lighting up one corner of the deep vastness of mink space. Throughout its life, the organism is primarily concerned about getting up to speed and little discerning and discriminating in assimilating new information. Acquired customs don’t need to be perfect, just good enough for survival. Over-imitation, copy-pasting of entire building blocks of behavior and reasoning, allows for fast dissemination of accumulated expertise but put a first ugly dent in our perception of uniqueness and originality. The concept of malleability dealt another harsh blow to the notion of the agent’s free will. The organism reacts to the current state of ex-org and in-org world points where in in-org world points contain the amassed stack knowledge. The infinite number of world points makes the future unpredictable but nonetheless predestined by prevailing conditions. Habits remove large number of decisions from the observer’s sphere of influence. They are automated sub-routines that don’t require active intervention, but are entire cloud bands that run independently from the here-now. Routines free up attention for unforeseen events by outsourcing well-known circumstances. At the same time, they introduce sturdiness into the thought process that can result in misalignments with real conditions especially in combination with over-imitation.

All the above doesn’t bode well for homo economicus; a view according to which we are rational beings, independent in their thinking and aware of their decision making at every step. In a Bohmian tradition, the mink-space framework integrated emotions into the thought process. We saw how their role for steering the organism on its equilibrium world line and how they contribute to memory formation and retrieval through compagination. We were also able to include dreaming to a wider range of thought patterns; sensory transmission determines the layout of thought chains in mink space (inbound/outbound). Different fields of thought were attributed to different personalities and types of intelligence. Finally, we tried to bridge the gap between this theoretical framework and the physical brain in a very raffish way. In this essay, we took a new look at age-old questions associated with the human mind. Maybe the reader could this discover one or the new element or inspiration. In the end, we believe that the merit of this approach lies not so much in the telling something new but in its simplicity and the elegant way it’s capable to bring together multiple largely isolated strands of investigation under a common doctrine. If this essay somehow made you question preconceptions of your umwelt by modifying your field of thought, it has more than achieved its objective.
