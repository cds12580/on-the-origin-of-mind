On the Origin of Mind

A Matter of Space and Time

Introduction. This essay takes a different kind of approach to fathom the evolution of mind from its origins to the planet’s most complex creation, the human brain. It assigns the dimension of time a key role for understanding the evolutionary forces at work.

An extended Minkowski space is mapped to event matrices that group concurrent event points into distinctive patterns. These patterns can be adjusted (learning), matched (analogy), complemented (reasoning), and concatenated (extrapolation). Thus, the emergence of intelligent life is reduced to a few simple, general rules.

The end of the analysis tackles some unanswered questions about the brain. We don’t know yet for certain why humans dream and why they forget. This analysis offers unconventional explanations by assigning a variable cost to the maintenance of memory.

Definition of life. In this framework we define life as any organized arrangement of matter that possesses a time horizon greater than zero. Inanimate matter has a time horizon of zero. We declare an organism as dead when its field of consciousness, defined as the ambit of events an organism can observe, collapses and it decomposes into individual particles of time horizon zero. This of course is a very broad definition of life and it practice arbitrary values must be defined.

Minkowski space. Minkowski space adds time as a fourth dimension to three-dimensional Euclidian space. The here-now is the locus where the world line intersects the present plane that separates the past from the future. It constitutes the origin of spacetime for the observer. The world line is the path the observer traces in Minkowski space. The here-now is also the world point where the observers past and future light cone meet. Events within the light cone are timelike; that is, events in the past light cone could have an effect on the here-now and events in the future light cone could be affected by the here-now.

Field of consciousness. Evolution brought forth organisms that were able to penetrate their environment deeper and deeper with their senses. As the sensory perception of space increased, so did the understanding of the impalpable dimension of time in classical co-evolution. Memory performance allowed extrapolating past events into the future. In a first evolutionary step, simple organisms developed a sense of time barely outside the here-now: A microbe might sense a chemical attractor in its proximity or a plant heat on its surface coming from a specific direction. As life evolved, a broader time horizon turned out to be a valuable faculty for a growing number of living beings on earth; animals could avoid obstacles when navigating physical space or chase nutrients in their proximity; herbivores could remember the most abundant feeding grounds and predators could prefigure the trajectory of their prey. Malcom MacIver argues that eyesight was crucial in this development. In a lovely video he shows how black ghost knifefish almost bypass their prey before hastening back to catch it; thus illustrating beautifully what a near-zero time horizon looks like.

As time horizon deepened, the field of consciousness grew exponentially and the number of possible events the observer could contemplate and track over time rose quickly. The brain emerged as a dedicated organ for time-related tasks. More tracked events mean an increased likelihood that any projection actually falls into the future light cone of the observer; hence, improving his chance of survival. Because the new organ is mainly a dynamic tool, moving objects are more prone to be detected than motionless; organisms will spot the moving mouse in a pile of leaves but won’t pay much attention to the single leave. The new organ also comes at an energetic cost and more contemplated states don’t necessarily mean a better overall outcome. The probability that a future event actually materializes decreases rapidly with its distance from the here-now. Therefore, any valid combination of need and readiness is good enough in Darwin’s terms. For a highly specialized animal like the black ghost knifefish, that mainly moves in one direction in a uniform environment under a much reduced visibility, a small time horizon simply proves to be sufficient.

Movement. Just like the brain tracks movement of external events regarding the frame of reference, it also tracks the movement of the organism itself. The body is just another agglomeration of traceable world points. By giving instructions to those world points, the brain determines the movements of those parts and consequently writes our trajectory in timespace (worldline). For a changing position, its time derivative is its velocity and its second derivative is its acceleration. Many other fundamental quantities in science are also time derivatives of one another: force is the time derivative of momentum, power is the time derivative of energy, and electric current is the time derivative of electric charge. Since all these quantities are heavily involved in the interconnection between brain and muscles we can clearly sense that time plays a crucial role for movement.

By observation and comparison of states, a newborn learns to make sense of the world and shove its body into the desired direction. Trial and error pair cause and effect in a way that can later be retrieved and rearranged in a planned manner: As it learns that lifting its right leg causes it to fall forward and to the right, lifting its left arm draws it to the left, and straightening the leg interrupts the fall, it can use all these orders in sequences when it actually want to move forward. Likewise, the small black ghost knifefish learns that wielding its fin makes him move either in this direction or that. Once memorized this sequence of events it can later be applied to chase its prey. The organism uses its temporal sense to track events and to adjust its own worldline accordingly to either avoid an undesirable collision (obstacle, predator) or to bring about a desirable one (food, shelter). Daniel Wolpert argues that our brain’s main purpose is to produce adaptable and complex movements because they are the only way to affect the world around us. This model integrates smoothly with Edmund Husserl’s theorem of intentionality, Karl Friston’s free energy principle, and Andy Clark’s concept of predictive processing.

Emotions. In this framework emotions can be interpreted as stimuli that nudge us into the right direction when we are not on track to reach the objective. The brain continuously computes a variance analysis and steers us towards the equilibrium path if the actual comparison is off target. Stress and insomnia indicate that we are behind the planned trajectory, fear that we cannot avoid an undesirable collision, and anger that we missed the goal. Joy on the other hand is the compensation for being on track or a met target. Sadness and frustration may imply that we are aware that we are unable to attain a certain desirable goal. Anger and frustration shall prevent us from repeating the same mistakes, but we can observe huge cultural differences in its elasticity leading to completely different equilibrium states. While a Japanese samurai might take his life for wrong life decisions, other cultures have a high tolerance for undesired consequences and behavioral changes in the future are unlikely. To care is a precondition for change and worries are the price of progress. Surprise and anticipation reflect unexpected and expected developments and help us adjust future forecasts. Fun is a learning strategy in an uncritical environment where a projected trajectory takes an unforeseen (comical) turn. A toddler may laugh when his parent disappears or emerges behind a chair. The same experience can proof critical when expecting life-threatening animals in high grass. Thus it constitutes a pleasant learning strategy. Trust and distrust are associated with other living beings that behaved according to our expectations in the past. Love ties us to advantageous world points and hate repels us from detrimental ones.

Emotions constitute the broadband antibiotic of thought, the fallback strategy in absence of other more specialized instructions. Conversely, we can infer that thoughts are evolved and more fine-tuned emotions that help us to develop more sophisticated strategies to navigate the targets and obstacles while constructing our worldline. Intuition would fall into the middle ground between emotions and thoughts. Unconscious emotions and conscious thought thus both work towards the same end of guiding us on the equilibrium path to reach our goals. Sudden and involuntary emotions are an important strategy for time-critical decision making in the here-now. They assure the organism’s immediate readiness for tasks that cannot wait. Thoughts on the other hand, handle unknown situations and long-term objectives both of which are almost unique to the human race. The field of consciousness is larger for our species because our potential field of operation is too. Occasionally, short-term and long-term strategies don’t align and trade-offs occur.


Human advantage. No other animal took more advantage of the evolutionary discovery of intertemporal comparisons than humans. The ability to draw knowledge from past events, to detect causalities and make predictions about future events turned out to be nature’s sharpest weapon yet. A sense for chronological order proved to be an indispensable prerequisite to make sense of the world. Suddenly static snapshots turned into life and we could track their development. A series of disconnected world points gave rise to a differential analysis where different states could be compared and discrepancies among states could be discovered. Self-consciousness and compassion were an unplanned brainchild of this same development in a very Darwinian manner. From now on, a new-born was no longer just a new-born and remained a new-born in the eye of the observer. An old man was no longer an old man out of nowhere.

Self-awareness. When the first men began to study their fellow creatures, it was merely an exercise that enabled them to learn from foreign mistakes or to anticipate unknown situations. Soon later and fully unintentional, observation developed into empathy. Compassion proved to be a success strategy for society as a whole. In a similar way, observation lead to self-reflection; if that baby whose development I tracked, became a boy and then a man, then I too was a baby at one point in the past. The seed of self-awareness had just been planted. And for the first time, thoughts of decay and the cycle of life entered the realm of humanity. Intertemporal comparison of formerly isolated states thus constitutes the foundation of thought and personality.

Energy demand. The human brain consumes up to 20% of the overall energy intake while only accounting for 2% of body mass; more than in any species on earth. Means must be found to compensate for those elevated energy needs: Just as predators followed their prey and predicted its next move, humans tracked the life cycle of crop and livestock over much longer periods of time. Tracking the life cycle of plants and livestock and extrapolating those observations offered the necessary calories to compensate for the brain’s elevated energy demand. It takes foresight and confidence that a certain event will materialize in the future to plant a seed in the here-now and harvest its fruits months later. Richard Wrangham argues that the advent of cooking improved the energy provision per meal even further. Pre-processing food outside the body breaks up tightly packed and inaccessible enzymes increasing energy density while reducing time and effort for chewing and digesting at the same time. Once again, studying causality and comparing the states of before and after – this time the effect of heat on food – gave the human race a critical edge. At one point, this reciprocal relationship in which energy offered foresight, and foresight offered energy, became self-reinforcing, letting the human race diverge from all other primates.

Speech. The tracking of objects was not restricted to a single sensory input. Little effort was needed for the brain to establish cross-sensory relations. Soon, organisms were able to connect signals coming from the visible spectrum caused by lightning with signals received from the audible frequencies caused by thunder. Equally, the odor of burning matter was paired with the light emitted by flames.

Cross-sensorial state causality also played a crucial role for the development of speech. If a zebra is bitten by a lion it will neigh in agony. The same sound will startle the herd whenever it hears it again. Both events became unequivocally intertwined. Humans learn language in a very similar way: we pin actions or pictures to sounds that follow (sometimes almost concurrently). A parent will point to a picture of a cow or reproduce its sound while repeating its name. The same association is later evoked when listening, speaking or reading. Hence, the correlation must be grounded – using the terminology of Terrence Deacon – in the here-now before it can be applied to events outside our current world point. All information pairing must take place in the here-now during the process of learning. Pairs of information are not restricted to be two-dimensional; they can consist of multidimensional vectors. Horses can be represented by the living animal, a drawing, a written word, or a sound pattern. Nor must these pairings be unique one-to-one mappings; one word can be assigned to multiple objects and one object can be assigned multiple words. The mappings are subject to morph, and meaning, spelling or pronunciation can shift over the course of time.

Language. Evolution gave rise to biodiversity and increasingly more and better adapted species unfolded. In a similar manner, language evolved and brought forth ever more fine-grained and better adapted terms. Every language is closely linked to its natural habitat. The term for mango could only emerge where mangoes grew and not all languages had a word for snow. At a later stage we added an additional layer of abstraction to our vocabulary. Now we could not only track physical objects in timespace but also immaterial events like love, freedom or happiness. In the here-now, we can make us perfectly understood by pointing and enacting; everything is sufficiently explained by just doing and showing. If the father feed a strawberry to his child in front of his wife all parties and actions are well-known to all observers. The same instant the action escapes timespace origin this is no longer the case. Edmund Husserl calls this proper and improper presenting. For improper presenting, all things and actions involved must be named unequivocally – father, child, feeding, and strawberry. Furthermore, subject, direct, and indirect object must be made clear. Time is the prerequisite for all that. The German word for verb is Zeitwort, word of time; without time there would be no action or movement, just frozen scenery.


Grammar. In essence, a developed sense of time is the most distinguishing feature of the human race and language is the means to situate events anywhere in spacetime. All languages possess more or less the same features. We use local prepositions to denominate the place of action. Grammatical tenses place events on the arrow of time and establish a chronological order. Subjunctive and conditional moods describe the desirable or likely trajectory of tracked material and immaterial world points in four-dimensional space. Languages evolved in a descriptive manner; trial and error led to more sophisticated ways to describe events outside the here-now. From time to time, linguistic scholars pruned and expanded natural developments into canonical order. Systematic grammar lifted language into states of lower entropy. Those episodes could only have occurred in a prescriptive way. Common rules facilitated communication; agreed-upon sentence structures made it easier to anticipate the next sound bite and pre-adjust hearing.

Transferability of experience. Even before the advent of language, humans were able to extrapolate events into the past and into the future well beyond the trajectory of their own world line. Theoretically, conjectures like those made by Charles Darwin would have been possible to obtain by a single person. Nonetheless, it would have been much more unlikely. All necessary data points like fossils, geological formations, and living creatures had to be located in physical proximity to the observer’s world line. Language made fields of consciousness transferable. Traced events and causalities could be passed on from person to person. Now the single observer could draw his information from the combined knowledge of the population. Merlin Donald calls these formations distributed cognitive networks. Above figure illustrates this relationship; solid dots represent events collected by the observer through his own senses and future projections made possible based on those same events. Empty dots illustrate such events that the observer acquired from other observers through language as well as extrapolations that were made possible through the combined knowledge of both own and foreign data points. The impact of speech cannot be overstated as it led to exponential growth of data points and thought complexity. In Integrated Information Theory proposed by Giulio Tononi, phi denotes the amount of integrated information in the system. The transferability of knowledge can explain why phi of the human race is by orders of magnitude larger than that of any other species. Phi can be seen as a measure of the possible number of world points and possible junctions for any given observer (figure 4). It is thus a monotonous function of the world space his horizon encompasses in all dimensions. Those are not limited to the four dimensions of Minkowski space but can be extended to other sensory information as smell, hearing, touch, taste or electric signals in the case of the black ghost knifefish.


Super-organisms. Society is a large group of individuals who live together in an organized way, making decisions about how to do things and sharing the work that needs to be done. Culture is the intellectual achievement regarded collectively and the way of life, especially the general customs and beliefs, of a particular group of people at a particular time. Proximity in spacetime is crucial for the emergence of culture as figure 5 illustrates. The depiction of super-organisms in timespace shows great similarities with migration paths of peoples on earth’s surface. Plurality is considered an advantage; many high cultures formed at the intersection of important trade routes. A broader field of consciousness raises the possible gamut of horizontal patterns (see section on thought patterns) for the super-organism and results in higher cognition. The field of consciousness of individual members must overlap as learning can only take place in the here-now. Information is paired into vectors. Some of its components always consist of soft information; information that goes extinct with the death of its bearer. It is the private key to decipher the public key consisting of hard information. It is the flame that must be kept alive and passed on from candle to candle. Just as shape and color of the flame change with chemical composition of the carrier, so does the connotation of soft information. The use, meaning, spelling and pronunciation of words morph over the course of time. Soft information builds up continuously; this holds true for the individual organism as well as the super-organism. It needs time to be created and cannot be acquired overnight or transferred one-to-one. Soft information is also hard to put into words; this is why people prefer to surround themselves with like-minded individuals. Members of a society share the same cultural background and hence a bigger overlap of soft-information vectors. Xenophobic attitudes emerge partially from a lack of understanding and intracultural communication barriers caused by differences in soft information.

As the carrier dies, so does connotation; the more carriers are alive the higher the probability that the soft part of a paired information is passed on. Irony has it that only dead matter in the form of artefacts survives the passage of time and only hard information is preserved, but context gets lost. Never will we have a complete picture of bygone cultures. We can never know for sure how Romans talked or Mayans perceived their world. Even if we could reconstruct soft information it would only be a snapshot within a dynamic process itself. Jamie Tehrani from Durham University illustrates how culture evolves on the example of storytelling. Storytelling is a highly efficient means of memorization owing to the high degree of vertical overlap where one picture leads to the next (vector depth; more on this in the section on event matrices) and the richness of details (vector breadth, one description might stick with one listener, another with another). From this perspective, religion is the most successful of all stories; a state-sponsored and orally transmitted set of laws, rules, best practices, experiences, scientific discoveries and entertainment.

In a living society, one individual can draw from the experience of other members as long as the event falls into its field of consciousness. The superset of society is mankind. In practice, we are influenced by much smaller subsets of society: our family and friends, the school we visit, the newspapers we read, people that speak our language. The super-organism gains from different thought patterns across its population. It would not make sense for each individual member to specialize in the future-heavy and extremely complicated task of forecasting tomorrow’s weather or the effects of climate change. But it does make sense to assign a small fraction of society to dedicate their time to do exactly that, so that other members who’s focus is much closer to the here-now can fulfill their task with more ease: The pilot who needs to bring her passengers safely to their destination or the restaurant manager who runs the outdoor beer garden where people enjoy the present moment. Just as the individual organism is much more dependent on vital functions in the here-now, so is the super-organism. An organism might survive without a functioning prefrontal cortex but it will fail immediately if it is deprived of its brainstem or the cerebellum. We can witness a related body response in the case of ego depletion. When confronted with scarce resources, long-term goals are sacrificed for the good of survival. There would be no tomorrow if the organism doesn’t survive today. Similarly, a super-organism formed of hunter-gatherers is perfectly fit for survival, whereas a super-organism composed solely of theoretical scientists or investment bankers is not. They would starve to death in less than a week’s time when left to their own devices in the wilderness. The dependence is completely one-sided; processes that are rooted deeper in evolution are a necessary precondition for more elaborate features. Thus, specialization and division of labor substantially extend the field of consciousness in high-order societies. In a low-order society with little integration, maximum phi is insignificantly larger than the maximum value achieved by any of its members and event vectors are shorter than in a super-organism of higher order. When placed in a functioning low-order society, a highly skilled individual could contribute very little to the overall wellbeing of the community and would have to downscale her skills to the least common denominator.

Free will. Within this framework, living organisms keep track of their environment within their field of consciousness. They make choices based on the next most likely state of the universe according to their projections of past events. This puts any decision into an uninterrupted chain of events governed by a simple algorithm of if this, than (most likely) that. Thus, Laplace’s demon inexorably appears before us and it seems difficult to escape the spell of causal determinism. If thoughts and emotions are nature’s way to steer us towards the equilibrium path, can we actually elude those impulses? It appears that in the end, free will might turn out an illusion, as every counteraction we undertake would have been produced by the same system we wanted to trick in the first place. All life events would be conditioned in every state. Even if life is completely predetermined it remains completely unpredictable due to the large number of interactions.

Malleability. Malleability tends towards zero as events approach the here-now. Think of the future light cone as a funnel where degrees of freedom are increasingly limited as the worldline advances; elements get compacted like soil particles in the ground. Those at the bottom of the stack hardly move. Same holds true for those decisions closest to the here-now: We have no control over bodily functions like heartbeat. Other activities are internalized in such a way that they don’t require attention but are executed unconsciously. These behavioral patterns are hardest to change; it takes weeks or month to unlearn how to ride a bicycle. The flow-state of mind is a condition in which the observer artificially curtails his field of consciousness; every sensory input triggers an immediate and unmitigated reaction. It is the most animal-like we can become; and most likely it feels so gratifying to the observer for this same reason. It is the closest we can come to our natural evolutionary roots. Malleability increases for higher-order decisions and larger time horizons.

Both hard (world points observable by senses; in more detail below) and soft (connotation inside the brain) information compose an event vector. Any evoked world point will trigger the associated event matrix and set off a chain reaction of overlapping matrices. We have little influence on the world points that hit our sensory organs in the here-now. At the same time, hard information initiates a mental process we have no control over. In order to change our habits successfully in the long run, we must change the elements of hard information we serve up to our sensory inputs by changing our surroundings. Again, any decision to steer us in one direction or another is taken by the same system we intend to change and malleability is not to be confused with free will. A whole section will be dedicated to event matrices and their components, horizontal and vertical event vectors.

Event matrix. By contemplating her field of consciousness, the observer stores world points into vertical and horizontal event vectors. Vertical event vectors are such that track a single event from a single sensory input along its temporal development. A horizontal vector pairs co-occurring events across different dimensions for a given present plane. Vertical event vectors are a didactic simplification as they allow collapsing three-dimensional Euclidian space into a single dimension. An example of a vertical vector would be the trajectory of football through the air. All snapshots are paired into a single vector; as the ball follows its journey, vector elements are populated. A sudden gust of wind might cause the flight path to deviate upwards but additional observations will make the necessary corrections to the vector string. Synapses are strengthened or depreciated. Also, possible gaps are filled that escaped our senses because we were distracted or the view was obscured by trees. The final result will be a perfect description of an elliptic ball trajectory under normal circumstances. At retrieval the mind can complement the missing vector elements from the model and extrapolate the flight path into the past or the future. In a similar fashion, horizontal vectors peg co-occurring world events into a single pattern. This will couple different flight patterns to varying surrounding conditions. Additional sensory inputs like moving leaves or wind pressure on the skin will be coupled to specific ball trajectories. Just as for vertical vectors, irrelevant elements might get added in the first run and relevant inputs might be omitted. But in the long run valid causality emerges as the corresponding synapses are re-enforced or dropped and the final structure crystalizes. Vector length varies during the learning process; a process that never actually comes to a halt. The brain pays special attention to changing world points relative to the frame of reference of the observer. Change is more likely to bring about change; and the brain is the tool to detect those dynamic changes in accordance with Weber-Fechner’s logarithmic perception of stimuli. The separation into vertical in horizontal components is fictitious and serves the purpose of illustration. They can be joined into an extended Minkowski space that includes all sensory dimensions. These additional axes are dependent on the characteristics of the observer. As the observer progresses along her world line unique patterns emerge in her field of consciousness (event matrix). The brain registers those changes and acts accordingly. Cross-sensorial event pairings are crucial for the emergence of speech as we will see in the next section.

Hard information is the information that is available as sensory input to the organism. Soft information is the connotation paired to hard information in event vectors inside the brain. Soft information dies with its bearer and is subject to morphing and forgetting. As new patterns are learned, the brain is in the dark to which other world points are decisive for its occurrence. Inconsequential data points are stored while consequential ones might be omitted. As the pattern repeats itself in the learning process, event matrices are superimposed and a dominant pattern emerges. Learning the same pattern can take place under different surrounding conditions for different observers. One child might learn to play football on the same sports ground amidst a pine forest. Another child might visit her grandmother every week in her flat with characteristic smell. In both cases the smell dimension will be logged as a relevant input to the event matrix while they are not generally considered to be essential for the specific activity. Thus “playing football” and “visiting grandmother” receive a personal connotation specific to the individual agent which can comprise bodily states as feelings and emotions. Meaning is part of the soft information and formed by personal experience; soft information can morph during the lifetime of the organism and shapes his survival strategy in evolutionary terms. Soft information is often deeply engrained in the organism and affects core instincts like taste; nothing tastes as good as childhood dishes. I, for example, like seafood but do not share the same passion for it as my Spanish friends who grew up near the coast.

The brain registers concurrent events and parts from a general premise of a relationship and causality among them. Concurrent event points are stored into the event matrix. Over time a prevailing a pattern emerges as relevant world points are strengthened and irrelevant ones fade and are eventually pruned. This process is never finished and the event matrix is subject to permanent change. Nevertheless, morphing slows down as a lignification process sets in and fundamental changes become increasingly unlikely. Observed event patterns are used to complement and solve unknown situations: A new event stimulates neurons connected to the sensory input; if the resulting event pattern is sufficiently similar to a stored observation, the remaining event points associated with the event are triggered too. If we spot a word of certain length and typical letter groupings, the rest of the word is filled in subconsciously which can even result in misreads. If we hear the words “Once upon a time…” the brain complements the event string. Thus, cognition is based on an infinite association game. All complex reasoning inside the brain can be reduced to a simple algorithm: snatch, match, and patch. Event patterns are observed by the senses and complemented on-the-go with comparable patterns from previous experiences. In this process the brain can be tricked into seeing things that aren’t there. Optical illusions ensue when the brain precipitates itself in the phase of matching and patching. The other morning my bed sheet had folded in such a way that an elongated shadow appeared along the crease ending in a head-like thickening. I reached out for it because I thought it was the loose end of my USB charger which often takes a similar shape in the same place. My brain had populated the real-world event pattern, nothing more than a rough shadow outline, populated with such richness in detail that even the individual connector pins were visible before my interior eye.

One event pattern sets of a sequence of overlapping patterns if given sufficient overlap among them. This process of reasoning is what we commonly refer to a as chain of thought (I prefer the expression chain of thought over the expression train of thought as the figure of speech carries the essential component of overlap). Any chain of thought originates from sensory input in the here-now, and the brain will suffer withdrawal symptoms if deprived of sensory stimuli resulting in hallucinations and mental illnesses under the conditions of solitary confinement. Different matrices associated with an event can be combined in matrix operations to generate new outcomes. In our example from above, the observer might project the trajectory of a flying football. If he observes wind at the same, the wind matrix might contain a contradicting motion pattern. Experience tells the brain that wind lifts light objects and presses them in the direction of air flow. Therefore the event will incorporate both matrices and the trajectory will be adjusted for both settings. As for the entanglement of an event with its surrounding, we can go as far to say that all information is context dependent. There cannot be any 1x1-event matrix; all information occurs in world-point clusters along the worldline. One night I was studying in my garret when I spotted a strange object in the dark sky. It was blinking and therefore no star, it wasn’t moving and therefore no airplane, it was too bright and therefore no satellite. Until this day I have no idea what I saw (a weather balloon, maybe). Yet the object is not an isolated object in my memories. It is pegged to the strange-things vector and to the local and temporal environment.

Extended mind. All thought originates in the here-now with the intake of new information regardless how negligible the observation might seem. One might think of the accounts of prisoners that put meaning into the dance of dust grains in the air. Even dreams are inspired by recent events (see section below). Observable world points (hard information) and meaning (soft information) are stored into vectors of variable length. If a vector element is called the whole column is activated. Therefore any world point in the observer’s field of consciousness forms part of her extended mind; a concept described by Andy Clark – even if not to its full extend. Out of sight, out of mind explains how the proverbial knot in one’s handkerchief can work: the information is tied to the knot and paired in the here-now. When the handkerchief is needed at some future point in time, the soft in formation is triggered as the event matrix is evoked. As a secondary effect, repetition increases the likelihood for the new information to be passed on into long-term memory (see below). Handwritten notes and arithmetic serve as memory aids in the same way; soft information is returned as hard information and its reading triggers the soft information originally stored with it. Notes are conscious brain output pegged to the event vector. Other information finds its way into the event vector way by much more subtle means as the football and grandmother example have shown. Vector length has a huge impact on memory performance and can provide clues why Alzheimer’s disease. A study among nuns has shown that mentally active nuns had shown fewer signs of memory loss than their mentally inactive peers. Higher mental activity results in longer event vectors and higher integration and redundancy. That is, if one vector element fails, more elements are left that evoke the same event. By the same token, the whole event vector is more likely to be refreshed and kept alive (see section on cost of storage). An active mind entails more elements of soft information in the event vector, e.g. the connotation in various languages. More elements of hard information can have a similar beneficial effect. Alzheimer patients removed from their familiar surroundings are deprived of hard-information world points. Those world points help triggering event points and refresh the memory. Hard-information vector elements cast doubts on the likely success of efforts to conserve the brain and revive it as technology catches up: Imagine an environment in which all hard world points have been swapped and were unrecognizable for the revived brain. The situation would be comparable to a prisoner in an empty cell suffering from a lack of outside triggers. Therefore, for the transplant to be successful not only soft information would have to conserved but hard information too. Otherwise, the cognition process would break down due to a prevalence of false-negatives. The pairing of hard and soft information is not limited to world points outside the organism. Emotions are tied to distinct facial expressions; certain feelings provoke specific facial muscles to react. Thus, soft information in the event matrix causes the remaining hard event points to fire up. The relationship works also the other way around. As we try to read the emotions of our peers we, try on their facial expressions. Bringing the facial muscles into position, we recreate the hard elements of the event matrix and the soft elements follow.

The sub-conscious. Many times I approach the entrance door to my apartment building, I find myself holding the right key already in my hands. The physical surrounding of a known environment triggers event points commonly associated with the event pattern. By the time I’ve reached the doorstep to my apartment on the fourth floor, my fingers have already moved forward to the right key, again. When leaving the apartment or moving away from the building, I don’t reach for my keys as I usually just close the door without locking it. Therefore, satisfying the horizontal threshold for the horizontal event vector is only a necessary condition for triggering the action associated with the event matrix. The vertical component must be met too. These sub-conscious event patterns can account for classical findings in psychological studies like the soap-dispenser that makes people become more conservative in their choices. The constant stream of pattern detection works flawless for most of the time. But even the brain can be tricked into false assumptions about the current situation. Flashbacks occur because irrelevant world points were pegged to the event vector and sufficient matrix points are met to trigger a past experience that is not essential to handle the present task at hand. Déjà-vus are false-positive pattern recognitions where the number of observed world points surpasses a critical threshold that leads to misfiring of associated matrix elements. They are therefore the Trojan horse of event patterns; the overall pattern is different but composed from the same underlying building blocks.

Thought patterns. We can classify thoughts by their distance from the here-now and the interconnectedness to it. These thought patterns in Minkowski space correspond roughly to the classification into gamma, beta, alpha, theta, and delta waves used in neuroscience (figure 7). Waves are a natural means for signal and data transmission. Different wave lengths come with different properties. Short wave lengths have higher frequencies and data rates but penetrate lesser distance as their energy gets dissipated faster. Longer waves travel longer distances at the expense of lower frequencies. At lower natural frequency, less energy is released per unit of time to the surrounding medium. Short wave lengths are associated with time-critical events that require strong focus in the here-now like playing a game of basketball. The Minkowski thought pattern is concentrated around the timespace origin and highly vertical (parallel to the worldline). Events are tracked and extrapolated into the future. Concentrated reasoning maintains a strong link to the here-now. The thought pattern takes the shape of an hourglass; events are tracked, processed, and returned for output to the here-now. Creativity is less time critical and the vertical component loses strength. Its Minkowski pattern now takes the shape of a radial polygon. Cross connections are made, some of them continue to run through the here-now. Douglas Hofstadter names analogy as the core of cognition. These horizontal links are the means by which we produce such analogies: They put us in the position to recognize patterns between seemingly unrelated events and to project the worldline of an observed on a new situation. Most of our best ideas come while doing simple but unrelated activities like walking or riding the bicycle. This is no coincidence. While conscious activities like reading demand constant connectivity to the here-now, those tasks are sufficiently automated and can be left unsupervised. At the same time, the activity is sufficiently demanding compared to sitting or lying not to fall asleep. Thus it allows highly productive cognitive thinking to kick in and be sustained for prolonged periods of time. The thought pattern can be visualized by a highly interwoven spatial mesh that is greatly detached from the here-now. Dream activity at night is most time insensitive; large waves waft in remote orbits from the here-now touching distant world points. We are mostly paralyzed while a small pilot light in the spacetime origin orchestrates basic biological functions. If this lights goes out we die, while long-lasting, low-frequency waves will likely continue to reverberate beyond the instance of death. Thus, the field of consciousness will collapse from the inside out the moment an organism dies. This visualization of thought patterns in timespace offers an elegant way to address the integration problem of dreaming raised by Jennifer Windt and Valdas Noreika.

Intelligence. The field of consciousness influences our personality. We can associate the depth of field and preponderant thought pattern with commonly used terminology regarding intelligence: motoric skills, social skills, problem solving, strategic thinking, abstract thinking etc. In absence of mental illnesses, all types of thinking are present in all people while absolute values within any given spectrum may differ. Some people might score higher for any range along the gamut. But more important in shaping personality seems to be the relative distribution along the spectrum. One could hastily assume that more data points necessarily increase one individual’s position in the evolutionary struggle. At first sight, more events in the observer’s field of consciousness increase the likelihood that more such events actually fall into the future light cone, and therefore contribute to better decision making. But for a higher number of events, more world points will be located at farther distances from timespace origin. This diminishes the likelihood that any such event actually will materialize at any given point in the future. From a Bayesian perspective it is easy to infer where I will be a second from now but very hard to predict where I will be after a decade or two. Indeed, as for a viable brain strategy there seems to be a sweet spot in the middle ground that is rewarded higher social recognition in terms of remuneration. This trade-off between number of data points and probability of occurrence guarantees that any relative distribution of thought patterns could outlast natural selection and fills its deserved place in society. The relative thought pattern lends any member of society her inherent comparative advantage and lets her specialize in what she does best. Thus society members complement each other and society as a whole behaves in a eusocial way. Due to the nature of the subject, activities close to timespace origin are rewarded or penalized immediately (think of the world cup final) while the true value (or faultiness) of abstract thought is oftentimes discovered only years after it had been expressed (one might think of the persecution and hate many great thinkers endured during their lifetimes).

Mental disorders. We could consider two edge cases in which the distribution function of the field of consciousness is considerably skewed towards either end of the spectrum (figure 8). A famous football player is generally considered one of the world’s best in his sport. Other players have shown similarly good performance in individual actions, but they do not show the same consistency as he does. At the same time this player shows an autistic awkwardness outside the pitch. Maybe it’s exactly the lack of distraction outside the here-now that contributes to his grandeur. He stays focused when the thoughts of his teammates wander away from the here-now and problems of their kids in school, quarrels with their wife, the opinion of the manager or of tomorrow’s press take center stage in the brain. John Nash Jr. on the other hand, was an American mathematician who made fundamental contributions to game theory. He also suffered from Asperger syndrome. Game theory encompasses decision making across all involved agents from the first move to the last. Maybe it was his lack of distraction in the here-now that let him focus on the big picture in novel ways.

Brain architecture. Before I had ever seen detailed illustration of the brain, my mental picture of the brain was that of a 3D maze where information could flow in any direction. It struck my mind that it actually was designed as a one-way street. This wasn’t what I had imagined beforehand. Every synapse has well defined entry points (dendrites) and exit points (axons); there are pre-synaptic neurons and there are post synaptic neurons. Much like the way we experience time on earth, there is always a clear before and after (feedforward). We observed a strong causality chain in the construction of the worldline; if this, than most likely that. We also detected a similar pattern governing the switch architecture of the brain. Learning teaches the brain from state A follows B, but there is no indication on how this process could be reversed for the purpose of retrieval. How can upstream elements of a vertical vector be filled in, or quasi-contemporaneous elements of a horizontal vector? The only way around this issue is to be as fast as light. To make this possible, A and B would have to become entangled at the moment of pairing.

Memory. When moving further away from timespace zero, the number of possible timespace events grows exponentially. It would be inefficient and unsustainable to log every potential state. The brain must balance its workload and a trade-off between information density and timespace distance is inevitable. Short wave lengths have a high frequency (transmission rate) and high information density whereas long wave lengths have a low frequency (transmission rate) and low information density. In the here-now I am totally aware of every letter I typed, which finger I lifted and every alternative thought that crossed my mind. Tomorrow I will remember only a fraction of that information, after one week less, and after one year almost nothing. Randomness acts as a gatekeeper to information storage; the more often an event occurs, the more likely it is that is important for our survival. Chances increase that it will be passed on into long-term storage. Closer to timespace zero, frequency is high and almost all information is passed on. Further away intervals get longer and randomness increases. Some irrelevant information might get stored right away, but in the long run, probability guarantees higher-quality outcomes. Effects of learning can be compared to a batter in baseball; practice will make him become more efficient over time, but individual results can vary strongly from play to play. The force he can transmit onto the ball is determined by split seconds outside of his control. The curvature of the bat will send the ball to the sky or to the ground; similarly the wave amplitude determines whether a signal is passed on strongly or not. The stronger the displacement amplitude, the more initial energy can be delivered over the individual vibrations over time, which of course lasts the longer the more initial energy is available and the less frequently an energy transfer can take place (frequency). As the amplitude diminishes memory fades. Repetition refreshes the impulse. Extraordinary memory abilities like the Savant syndrome could be explained by perfect synchronization or permanent refreshers.

Data compression. Humans possess a very large brain and studies show that we only use a small fraction of it. Still, scarcity seems to be an issue. We forget. Even with unlimited resources it would be an inefficient strategy to store any information as-is. Rather, it would be a good idea to find some small, reusable building blocks just like the letters in a sentence or nucleotides in our gene code. Let’s assume a complex signal enters our brain, then it would make sense to split it up into its base component which could be waves of pure frequencies, and store them with other frequencies of the same kind in dedicated areas: all visual signals together, all acoustical signals together and so on. If the memory is to be retrieved, the same signal could be recomposed as good as possible by reassembling simple waves into a complex super-positioned wave mimicking the original signal’s unique footprint. Something similar actually seems to happen in the brain where various regions are involved when processing information. Integrated Information Theory postulates a concept called ‘exclusion’ stating that parts of the brain have non-zero phi. Standing waves within neurons could account for such phenomena.

Cost of storage. Regardless of the efficiency of the storage algorithm and regardless of seemingly infinite storage capacity, the brain seems to constantly match supply with demand of information. Thus there must be some variable cost be associated with storing memory. Information doesn’t come preloaded but is only added when needed and must be arduously acquired in the process. A child does not know how to walk until it is practically able to do so. Which does make sense; if it was born on a ship or with only one leg, walking would require different skills under these specific circumstances. The way we must move our mouth muscles varies by the corner of the earth we were born into and the language we speak. Analogously, unused information is constantly purged from our memories. If memory only would be a chemical composition that doesn’t wither over time, we could simply drop it in some corner of the brain like a load of stones and leave it there. More always would be better. Both, the fact that memory doesn’t come preloaded (and, later adjusted if required) and that it gets purged when not needed, indicate that excess information is associated with some operational burden. One possible explanation could be that noise and resistance increase with the number of available synapses. Another explanation could be that memory needs to be actively refreshed in order to stay retrievable. Thus, more memories require more energy to retain. A further argument could be made that if memory was non-volatile and with the right technology at our disposal, we would be able to read out the memory of the dead. This seems implausible. The moment we die, our field of consciousness most likely implodes from the inside out as high frequency waves demand higher energy intake.

Dreams. Sleep is commonly regarded a feature reserved to higher developed species. Presence of a brain and sleep are thus closely linked. Higher developed species usually specialize into diurnal or nocturnal activity according to their senses. The remaining hours are dedicated to recovery. The daily cycle consequently constitutes a repeating rhythm of charge and discharge. As holds true for the rest of our body, we can safely assume that night sleep is dedicated to maintenance tasks for which no energy can be provided during wake hours. At day most thought activity is time-critical and strongly linked to the here-now. External stimuli create new impressions in the brain or strengthen existing ones. Existing memories are actively retrieved and applied to the current situation. When our thoughts strive away, the connection to the here-now gets lost and brain activity becomes more and more remote; we daydream. Brain waves propagate through existing paths starting from the point of last activity. At night this pattern is even more pronounced and the link to the here-now almost completely broken; only basic functions remain active. During sleep, waves slosh around aimlessly in the brain like water in a ship tank. More energy ripples through to synapses which are already better connected and therefore more important. If synapses actually host standing waves this could be a means to reenergize them and push important data further down the road into long-term memory. This explanatory approach thus falls in line with the role of dreaming for memory consolidation proposed by Chiara Cirelli and Giulio Tononi.

The process is more likely to kick off in areas of concentrated energy and originates where we left off; therefore, we frequently dream about recent events. Some memories will be refreshed while other will fade away and will finally be purged in deep sleep: important vector elements are re-enforced while such deemed irrelevant will finally get dropped (pruned). The maintenance work will free up resources and the saved energy can then be directed to those memories which proved to be vital in the past. Hence, dreaming keeps the outmost orbits of the field of consciousness energized. Sleep is more important for the young offspring because more such maintenance is needed to guarantee successful reproduction. It is also the period of biggest adaption while new impression will gradually decrease later in life. When any such dream activity hits unpleasant memories, it is likely that a thought escapes the remote pattern and turns inbound to the here-now because these events are closely tied to flight and fight responses; we wake up. Conversely, if our physical condition requires the attention of our brain during sleep, we are more likely to be steered into nightmares too; after an abundant and salty dinner at the Greek restaurant I often rudely awake as my body craves for water. The existence of such somatosensory stimulations has been shown in scientific studies.

The refresher function of dreaming also acts as preparedness vehicle for upcoming events. Next week we will visit my friend’s family for the second time after two years have elapsed. Naturally, we speak more about her family and episodes from the last vacation during the weeks before our trip; thus drawing brain activity towards those memories. There was this one instance which obviously surprised me and left a lasting impression with me: How could ice-cream be half as good and twice as expensive in a third-world country? We hadn’t talked about this particular event lately. Still, just talking about her nieces was enough to trigger the event matrix associated with our trip together to the ice-cream parlor. In my dream, the actors were interchanged and recent events from last week were mixed into the setting (two kids from last week’s class). The figures were secondary and only placeholders; the main message was the underlying pattern and the brain didn’t want to be taken by surprise another time. Actually, this is a repeating pattern throughout dreams; people aren’t well defined by their physical appearances, and still it is clear who it is because all the other elements of the matrix that define their character are present. The relevant world points of the event matrix define the element; different objects and agents can share subsets of the same matrix. The event is recognizable if a certain threshold is met. Dreams are than the concatenation of event patterns into a more or less logical chain of thoughts. A connection is logical if sufficient overlap is given between any two or more event matrices. If a dreamer awakes from a dream and only little time has elapsed, she is able to recall and reconstruct large parts of the dreams and establish the link to real-life impressions of the recent past because the associated brain regions are still reverberating. On an interesting side note, few observers report smells and flavors to appear in their dreams. Immanuel Kant, the German philosopher, called smell the "least rewarding and most easily dispensable" of our senses. Of dreamers that experience olfactory or gustatory dreams, many work in scent and taste-related industries. Smell events are therefore embedded in larger event matrices and the probability that any such world point is evoked by association in a dream increases rapidly. We can also expect that animal dreams correspond to the reliance on individual senses.

Time measurement. In our daily life we need repetitive phenomena in the universe to measure the passage of time. The earth for example, revolves around the sun and rotates around its own axis. It does so in a predictable fashion and both patterns are comparable to one another. Mankind used this fact by observing the sky and inventing sundials. Later we discovered that we could use harmonic oscillators in pendulum clocks and oscillating properties of quartz crystals for timekeeping. All behave in the same predictable way. It would be conceivable to assume that standing waves are involved in a similar way at the inside of living organisms to measure the passage of time. This form of time measurement could play a role for subconscious timing like biorhythm and motion but doesn’t seem to be accessible to active decision-making; we perform notoriously bad in estimating time and need additional means like counting etc. to make educated guesses.

Arrow of time. When it comes to the question of how the brain classifies world points into a chronological order two different factors are likely at play. On one hand memories fade, that is the amplitude of the wave signal diminishes. On the other hand all world points fit into one uninterrupted chain of events; a steady sequence of decision inputs and outputs (reflected in our neuron architecture). The former might work for very remote events but becomes increasingly unreliable as two world points come close to one another; even more so if both events are at long distances from the here-now. Relative distance seems to be an important factor. It looks like time sensibility follows a logarithmic scale in accordance with the laws of Weber-Fechner. The latter of both approaches seems to be a much more reliable mechanism to bring world points into a sensible chronological order whereby events are qualified by the inputs that went into them. Before the invention of the written word, storytelling was an efficient means to place a series of connected events on the arrow of time. When talking about various childhood vacations it is extremely difficult to locate them on the time axis in absolute terms. The way this is possible is usually through input-output reasoning: I remember that we were still living in our old home when visiting France and I remember that my uncle was with us in Italy when I told him about our holidays in Spain while sunbathing at the beach. We could argue that an event’s likelihood to be remembered increases with number of occasions it is used as input for other decisions. This input-output reasoning is also why we remember street corners better than the stretches in between; we took conscious decisions. During episodes of high alert, all attention is directed to the here-now and otherwise irrelevant details are registered. This augments the number of decisions for a fixed period of time. The higher than usual number of decisions stretches the relative component of time perception. This is what I experienced that night when we flipped our car on a California backroad in 1997: The curve literally seemed to be never-ending and the whole accident unfolded in super slow-motion before my eyes.

Integration with existing theories. This essay parted from the mind-body problem (hard question) and the lack of a convincing and comprehensive theory that could explain the various aspects and manifestations of intelligent life. I felt disappointed by dualistic escape routes that try to separate physical and mental properties into fundamentally different things. I believe that the extended spacetime framework has the potential to develop into a valuable and unifying tool for a wide range of disconnected disciplines from neurosciences, information theory, quantum computing to behavioral and social sciences. I hope that it reaches the eyes of the right people who might take up some of its core ideas and develop them further into a solid, coherent theory. The document is roughly structured from high to low confidence positions in the view of the author. The observations set forth in this article are far from perfect but satisfying enough for the author. Having assembled the various arguments along the way, he feels like a ten-year-old boy to whom was just explained how he can stream his favorite movie out of thin air. He will never fully comprehend how a living scene is filmed, stored, emitted, and reproduced; much less will he be able to rebuild the whole process or parts of it on his own. But he has a feeling that he has understood the big picture of how everything could come together.

Elements of this hypothesis connect seamlessly with existing theories on brain functions and consciousness. The definition of life used in this thought experiment puts it in the philosophical tradition of emergent properties. Malcom MacIver reasoned that eyesight led organisms to develop foresighted strategies. Daniel Wolpert argued that the brain’s main purpose was to command body motion in order to influence one’s environment. Douglas Hofstadter described analogy as the core of cognition. We interpreted analogies as horizontal thought patterns and saw that language extended the field of consciousness horizontally well beyond the close physical environment of our worldline. The spacetime framework also embraces panpsychism and Deacon’s description of vegetative sentience, as it justifies a quasi-steady path from inanimate matter to complex human consciousness. The sharing of fields of consciousness through language can be compared to the distributed cognitive networks proposed by Merlin Donald. These can explain the huge jump of phi from humans to their closest relatives. A concept brought forward by of Integrated Information Theory. In the present framework phi is zero when the time horizon is zero (dead matter). This model also integrates smoothly with Edmund Husserl’s theorem of intentionality, Karl Friston’s free energy principle, and Andy Clark’s concept of predictive processing. The incorporation of hard information is partially reflected in the concept of the extended mind proposed by Andy Clark and David Chalmers.
